📹 Zoom: pure talking game, cameras on. Teacher uses the ⏱ timer or counts down; use the 👏 reaction for every good "because".

How to play:

1. Your teacher names a silly position — for example: "Pizza is better cold."
2. You get 30 seconds to argue FOR it — even if you totally disagree! 😈
3. Then swap: YOU pick a silly position, your teacher defends it.

No score here — arguing for things you don't believe is exactly how real debaters train! 🧠

🎣 The hook

Imagine a classroom where nobody checks a screen for six whole hours. To some students, that sounds like a nightmare; to many teachers, it sounds like paradise. This year, our school is debating a total phone ban — and everyone has an opinion.

The student council has been collecting views for two weeks. Its box in the corridor is already stuffed with more than a hundred notes, and the debate has spread from the classrooms to the dinner tables of half the town.

📣 The case for a ban

Supporters make a strong claim: phones destroy concentration. According to one widely reported study, it can take over twenty minutes to fully refocus after a single notification. Furthermore, phones don't only distract their owners — one lit screen pulls the eyes of every student nearby.

In addition, there is the social argument. Break time used to mean conversation; now it often means thirty heads bent over thirty screens. As a result, some students say they feel lonelier at school than anywhere else.

Teachers tell a similar story. Ms Varga, who has taught maths here for twelve years, says the change is visible from the front of the room: "Ten years ago I competed with daydreaming. Now I compete with a casino in every pocket."

🔄 The other side

However, opponents of the ban raise fair points. Phones are not just toys: they are calculators, dictionaries and cameras. Moreover, parents want to reach their children in an emergency, and many students travel home alone.

Opponents also argue that a ban teaches the wrong lesson. Whereas adults are trusted to manage their own screen time, a total ban treats teenagers like small children. School, they say, should teach self-control — not simply remove the temptation.

There is a practical worry too. Who would collect three hundred phones every morning, guard them safely, and hand the right one back to the right student? A rule that cannot be enforced, opponents warn, quickly becomes a rule that nobody respects.

🥊 The rebuttal

Admittedly, phones can be useful tools. Nevertheless, the evidence suggests we are not using them as tools most of the time. One survey at our own school found that fewer than one in ten phone pick-ups during lessons were for school work.

Therefore, keeping phones in our pockets "just in case" means accepting hours of distraction in exchange for minutes of usefulness.

⚖️ The verdict

On balance, a full ban during lessons — with phones allowed at lunchtime and after school — seems the fairest compromise. It protects concentration where it matters most, while still respecting students' lives outside the classroom.

The school votes next month. Both sides are preparing posters, and the student council has promised that every vote — student or teacher — will count equally.

Whatever you believe, notice one thing: this article did not shout, insult anyone, or exaggerate. It made claims, offered evidence, faced the counter-argument, and answered it. That — not volume — is the art of persuasion.

Teacher note — Step 4 + answer key

Quiz answers: over twenty minutes · calculators, dictionaries and cameras · fewer than one in ten · a ban during lessons, phones free at lunch/after school. Open Q1 model: conceding shows fairness and honesty — the reader trusts a writer who has clearly considered both sides, and the counter-argument loses its surprise power. Open Q2: any position is fine; assess only whether two linkers are used correctly. Linker hunt (11): According to, Furthermore, In addition, As a result, However, Moreover, Whereas, Admittedly, Nevertheless, Therefore, On balance.
🔧 Language Focus — Linking Words & the Counter-Move 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. For each quiz item, student says the answer out loud BEFORE you click it.

🧱 BUILDING linkers

Adding: Furthermore, · Moreover, · In addition,
Result: Therefore, · As a result,
Evidence: According to…, · For instance,

Phones break focus. Therefore, they should wait in lockers.

⚔️ CONTRAST & CONCESSION linkers

Contrast: However, · On the other hand, · Whereas…
The golden pair:
Admittedly, phones help in emergencies.
Nevertheless, they can wait in a bag.
🏗️ The persuasion skeleton: 1️⃣ Hook → 2️⃣ Claim → 3️⃣ Evidence (Furthermore…) → 4️⃣ Concede the counter-argument (Admittedly…) → 5️⃣ Rebuttal (Nevertheless…) → 6️⃣ Call to action.

💡 Tip: conceding is a POWER move, not a weakness. "Admittedly X. Nevertheless Y." beats pretending the other side doesn't exist — it steals your opponent's best card before they can play it. 🃏

Teacher note — Step 5

Watch for the classic B2 error: using "Whereas" like "However" at the start of a lone sentence — Whereas needs TWO compared things in one sentence. If items 5–6 wobble, drill orally: "Whereas my brother ___, I ___" with three fillings. 6+/8 = bank time for the pitch in Step 6.

Teacher note — Step 6

The concede-and-rebut line is the assessed core — never skip it. Sample pitch ("School should start at 10:00"): claim: "Our school should start at ten." · args: "According to sleep researchers, teenage brains need more morning sleep. Furthermore, tired students learn less in the first two lessons." · counter: "Admittedly, a later start means a later finish. Nevertheless, one useful hour beats two sleepy ones." · CTA: "Sign the student council petition on Friday." Time the delivery; a 40-second confident pitch beats a 90-second mumble.
