🏛️ Inside the Grand Final — school report by Noor, 15

Last Friday, our school hall stopped being a place where people eat sandwiches and became a courtroom of ideas. Two teams reached the national final, and I had a press pass.

📜 The motion

Every debate begins with a motion — the statement the teams fight over. Friday's motion, which had been kept secret until one hour before, was: "Social media does more harm than good to teenagers."

The proposition team had to defend the motion. The opposition, whose captain was our own Lena Kovač, had to destroy it. Both teams got sixty minutes, one laptop and a mountain of nerves.

🎤 Opening statements

The proposition opened with a hook: "By the time you finish school today, you will have scrolled through more posts than your grandparents read newspapers in a year."

Strong start. However, Lena's opening was ice-cold: "It has been claimed that social media harms us. Interesting — that claim was posted… on social media." The hall actually gasped.

⚔️ The rebuttals

A rebuttal is the counter-attack: you take the other team's best argument and calmly take it apart. Lena's teammate Omar reminded everyone that banning things rarely works: "If prohibition had worked, my little brother would have stopped eating sugar years ago."

The proposition must have prepared for that line, because their answer came fast: regulation, not prohibition. The audience was on the edge of their seats.

🧑‍⚖️ The verdict

The judges praised both teams for avoiding getting personal and for remembering to attack ideas, never people. That rule is the heart of formal debate.

In the end, the opposition won by two points. Lena said the trophy belonged to the whole team, then carried it around the hall for an hour anyway.

💬 Why it matters

You may never enter a debating hall, but you will argue — with friends, parents, and one day, bosses. Debate teaches you to disagree without burning bridges.

Noor's tip if you want to try it: start small. Challenge a friend to a two-minute kitchen debate about breakfast cereal, and appoint a parent as judge.

As Lena put it: "Anyone can shout. Champions persuade."

Teacher note — Step 4 + answer key

Quiz answers: the statement the teams fight over · one hour before · the opposition (Lena's team) · attack ideas, never people. Open Q1 sample: shouting uses volume/emotion with no evidence; persuading uses arguments, evidence and calm language to change minds. Open Q2: any side + one real argument. Annotation key — reported: "It has been claimed that…"; relative clauses: "which had been kept secret…", "whose captain was…"; idioms: "on the edge of their seats", "burning bridges", "ice-cold". Bonus retrieval: the text also hides a future perfect ("will have scrolled"), a third conditional ("would have stopped"), a deduction ("must have prepared") and gerund/infinitive ("avoiding getting", "remembering to attack") — a scavenger hunt if time allows.

🧰 YOUR LEVEL 4 GRAMMAR TOOLBOX

S1 🕵️ must / might / can't have (deduction)
S2 ⏳ mixed conditionals, wishes
S3 🔗 who / which / whose clauses
S4 📰 "It has been reported that…"
S7 🔮 will have done / will be doing
S8 🌏 remember doing vs remember to do

🎤 YOUR LEVEL 4 SPEAKER TOOLBOX

S5 🎭 idioms & figurative language
S6 🗣️ arguments, linking words, counter-arguments
S9 🪧 hooks, discourse markers, pauses

In a debate you need BOTH boxes at once — that's why this is the final boss level. 👑
💡 Debate armour: a deduction proves you think ("The ban can't have worked…"), a conditional shows consequences ("If we banned phones, students would…"), and a marker keeps the judge with you ("However, consider this…").
