ЁЯжИ The photo that started everything

On a rainy Tuesday last October, a photo appeared on a local news page in the small town of Milford. It showed a large grey fin cutting through the water of Lake Harmon. Within an hour, the headline "SHARK SPOTTED IN LAKE HARMON" had been shared more than ten thousand times.

ЁЯУ░ What the reports said

The shark was said to be over three metres long. It was reported that a fisherman had seen it "with his own eyes", and one website even claimed that a dog had been chased out of the water.

By Wednesday morning, schools near the lake were said to be planning to cancel their swimming lessons. Panic travels fast.

By lunchtime, the story had crossed into other towns. The lake car park was reported to be full of shark-watchers with binoculars, and a local caf├й began selling "Shark Alert" hot chocolate. Nobody, however, had actually filmed the shark тАФ which was strange, in a town where everybody films everything.

ЁЯХ╡я╕П Enter the fact-checkers

At Milford High School, a media club led by fourteen-year-old Priya Nair decided to investigate. The club had been trained to ask three simple questions: Who published this? What is the evidence? Do other reliable sources agree?

The answers were worrying. The "local news page" had been created only two weeks earlier, the fisherman was never named, and no other newspaper had reported the story at all.

The club also looked at the page's other articles: a two-headed duck, a UFO over the supermarket, and a snowstorm predicted for July. A pattern was starting to appear.

ЁЯФН The truth comes out

Then came the breakthrough. Using a reverse image search, the club discovered that the photo had actually been taken in Australia in 2019 тАФ it is thought to have appeared in at least twelve fake stories since then.

And the "fin" in Lake Harmon? It was later confirmed to be a piece of black plastic pipe, placed in the water as a prank by two bored college students.

The students apologised, and the pipe was fished out of the lake in front of a small, slightly disappointed crowd. Swimming lessons continued as normal that Friday.

ЁЯТн Why it matters

The hoax was believed by thousands of people, partly because it felt exciting, and partly because everyone else was already sharing it. As Priya told a real journalist afterwards, "A lie doesn't need to be clever. It just needs to be shared faster than the truth."

Experts say that stories which trigger strong emotions тАФ fear, anger, excitement тАФ are shared far more often than calm, true ones. So next time a headline makes your heart race, pause. Ask who, ask what, ask where else. The shark you are afraid of might just be a plastic pipe.

Priya's club now runs a "Question It" noticeboard in the school corridor, where the fake story of the week is posted next to the three questions. It is said to be the most-read board in the whole school.
