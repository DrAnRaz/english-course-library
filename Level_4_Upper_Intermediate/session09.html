How to play:

1. Your teacher gives you a very serious topic… like socks 🧦, pizza crusts 🍕 or why Mondays exist.

🎤 How to speak so people listen — a survival guide for teen presenters

Last term, a quiet student named Dario had to present his science project to the whole year group. He was so nervous that he rehearsed in front of his goldfish. Two minutes into the real talk, something surprising happened: people were actually listening.

Dario is not a natural performer. He speaks quietly, he hates being watched, and he once faked a cough to escape a class presentation. If HE can hold a room for two minutes, the rest of us are out of excuses.

🪝 First of all: the hook

First of all, great speakers never begin with "So, um, my project is about…". Dario opened with a question instead: "What if I told you our school bin is a zoo?"

A hook can be a question, a shocking number, or a ten-second story. In other words, give the audience a reason to look up from their phones within the first sentence. Dario's bin question did exactly that.

The bin really was a zoo, too. His project counted seven kinds of insects living in it, plus one very confident mouse that the caretaker still refuses to discuss.

🪧 Secondly: signpost everything

Secondly, listeners get lost far more easily than readers, because they can't rewind you. Words like firstly, however, for instance and to sum up work like road signs through your talk.

Dario used exactly three: "I'll show you three creatures. Moreover, one of them lives in the yoghurt corner." The room went completely silent — in the good way.

🐢 Thirdly: slow down. No, slower.

Thirdly, nervous speakers rush. Your heart says sprint; your audience needs a walking pace and real pauses.

A pause after a big point feels terrifying to you but powerful to them. In fact, professionals plan their silences as carefully as their sentences. Count two seconds in your head, breathe, and only then move on.

Dario practised his pauses with a kitchen timer. His rule was simple: say the big sentence, look up, count "one banana, two bananas", continue. It felt ridiculous in his bedroom and looked masterful on stage.

👀 The body talks too

What's more, your body speaks before you do. Standing tall, looking at faces instead of shoes, and using your hands to show size and shape — all of this tells the room you believe your own words.

On the other hand, reading straight from your slides tells them you'd rather be somewhere else. Slides are your poster, not your script.

🏁 To sum up

To sum up: hook them early, signpost the path, slow your pace, and let your body do half the talking. Dario finished to genuine applause and one excellent question about yoghurt.

Three teachers asked him for his tips afterwards. He wrote them on a sticky note: question, road signs, bananas, hands. Nobody except Dario knows what "bananas" means, and that is exactly how legends begin.

His goldfish, sadly, has heard the talk forty times and remains unimpressed. Audiences are hard to please.

🔧 Language Focus — Discourse Markers: Your Road Signs 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. For each quiz item, student says the answer BEFORE you click it — with speaker voice and a gesture!

🗺️ MARKERS THAT BUILD THE PATH

Opening: first of all, to begin with, let me start with…
Adding: moreover, what's more, in addition, also
Example: for instance, for example, such as
Closing: to sum up, in conclusion, finally

🔀 MARKERS THAT CHANGE DIRECTION

Contrast: however, on the other hand, even so
Result: as a result, therefore, that's why
Rephrasing: in other words, put simply
Emphasis: in fact, above all, believe it or not
💡 The road-sign rule: one marker per turn, not per sentence. A talk with a marker in EVERY sentence sounds like a robot GPS. 🤖 Use them where the road actually turns.

💡 Tip: in speech, markers usually come FIRST in the sentence, followed by a tiny pause: "However, ⏸ there is a problem." That pause is where your audience catches up.

Teacher note — Step 6

Success = 4+ different markers, a real pause after each, and eye contact with the camera (not the screen corner). Film-director trick: have them deliver the hook TWICE — flat first, then "like it's a movie trailer". The second version is always transformed. Applaud loudly; this is many students' first real speech.
