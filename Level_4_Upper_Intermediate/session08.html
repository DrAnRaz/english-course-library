✈️ Culture shock diary — by Bruno, 15, from São Paulo

Two months ago I won a place on a student exchange to Osaka, Japan. I expected new food and a new school. I did not expect my brain to short-circuit quite so often.

📅 Week one: the rules nobody writes down

I'll never forget arriving at my host family's house. I walked straight in, and everyone froze — I had forgotten to take off my shoes at the door.

In Brazil we hug everyone, so I tried hugging my host mother. She turned into a statue of pure politeness. Now I remember to bow, and honestly, I like it.

🚇 Small things, big lessons

On the train, nobody talks on the phone. Ever. I stopped to answer a call from my mum, and a hundred silent passengers politely ignored my crime.

Since that day I have stopped using my phone on trains completely. My host brother Haruto says even his grandmother approved, which is apparently the highest honour in Osaka.

🍜 Food adventures

Eating here means learning new rules too. Slurping your noodles isn't rude — it's a compliment to the cook!

I regret not learning more Japanese before the trip, but food words came fast. Nobody forgets the word for "delicious" when the food is this good: oishii!

My host family also taught me to hold my rice bowl while eating, and never to stick my chopsticks upright in the rice. Apparently that is a serious taboo connected with funerals. I learned that one the hard way, in complete horrified silence.

💭 What culture shock really is

People think culture shock means hating the new place. It doesn't. It means your automatic habits suddenly stop working, and your brain has to drive in manual mode.

It's exhausting for two weeks. Then one morning you bow to your teacher without thinking, and manual mode quietly becomes automatic again.

🎌 My advice

If you ever go on an exchange, remember to watch before you act, and don't be afraid of making mistakes. Mine make the best stories now.

Haruto visits Brazil next year. I can't wait to watch HIS brain short-circuit when my whole family hugs him at the airport. All fourteen of us.

Quiz answers: take off his shoes · nobody talks on the phone · a compliment to the cook · about two weeks. 🔧 Grammar — Gerund vs Infinitive: The Meaning Changers 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. For each quiz item, student says the answer BEFORE you click it — and explains WHY in one short sentence.

⏪ VERB + GERUND (-ing) — looks BACK

The action already happened, or it's a habit you quit.

I remember arriving. (memory of the past)
I stopped using my phone. (quit the habit)
I'll never forget seeing it. (unforgettable memory)
I regret not learning Japanese. (sorry about the past)
Try slurping! (experiment — see what happens)

⏩ VERB + INFINITIVE (to…) — looks FORWARD

The action is a task, duty or purpose still ahead.

Remember to bow. (don't forget the task!)
I stopped to answer a call. (paused IN ORDER TO)
Don't forget to take off your shoes. (future duty)
We regret to tell you… (formal bad news)
Try to arrive on time. (make an effort)
💡 The time-arrow trick: gerund = the action points ⏪ backwards (already real). Infinitive = the action points ⏩ forwards (still to do). Same verb, opposite arrow, different story.

💡 Hungarian tip: Hungarian often uses ONE form for both ("elfelejtettem bezárni" = forgot the task AND the memory). English splits them: forgot to lock = the door is open! 😱 forgot locking = the door is fine, you just don't remember the moment.

✏️ Practice — tap the correct form

Teacher note — Step 5

Score 6+/8 → move on and bank time. Under 5 → redo misses with the time-arrow question: "Is the action behind you or ahead of you?" The hardest pair for most students is try + -ing (experiment) vs try + to (effort) — demo it physically: "Try TOUCHING your nose with your tongue" (experiment!) vs "Try TO reach the ceiling" (effort).

🏠 Homework

🌍 Ask one family member about a custom or tradition from their childhood that has changed or disappeared.

✏️ Write 4 sentences about it using: remember + -ing, stop + -ing, remember + to, and try + -ing.

📌 Example: "My grandad remembers writing real letters. People stopped sending them when email arrived. He says I should try writing one — so I must remember to buy a stamp."
