⚡ Warm-Up — Weekend Detective 5 minStep 2
📹 Zoom: pure talking game, cameras on. Use the 🎉 reaction every time the student makes a correct deduction.

How to play:

1. Your teacher gives 3 clues about their weekend (example: "On Sunday my shoes were muddy.").
2. You are the detective — guess what the teacher did! Start your guesses with "You must have…", "You might have…" or "You can't have…" 🕵️
3. Then swap: YOU give 3 clues about your weekend, and the teacher deduces.

No score here — just wake up your inner detective! 🧠

🕵️ The Case of the Missing Mascot — from the case files of Priya, Year 9 reporter

Last Friday, our school's mascot costume — Marvin the Moose — vanished from the sports hall storeroom. The basketball final was only two days away, and the whole school was in a panic. As lead reporter for the school paper, I decided to investigate.

🔍 The clues

The storeroom door hadn't been forced, so the thief must have had a key. Only four people carry one: Coach Dembélé, the caretaker Mr Novak, our team captain Alicia, and Dev from the drama club.

On the dusty floor I found small footprints — size 36, far too small for the coach or the caretaker. There was also a faint smell of fresh paint, and one shiny silver sequin.

I photographed everything and wrote each detail in my notebook. My journalism teacher always says that a good reporter records the facts first and forms opinions later, so that is exactly what I did.

🧩 The suspects

Alicia had a perfect alibi. She was at basketball practice all afternoon, in front of thirty witnesses. She can't have taken the costume, so I ruled her out immediately.

The adults were easy to check too. Coach Dembélé had left for an away match at lunchtime, and Mr Novak had spent the whole afternoon fixing a leaking pipe in the library — the librarian confirmed it. Neither of them could have visited the storeroom, and anyway, their feet are enormous. Two more suspects ruled out.

Dev, however, had been acting strangely. He had stayed late on Friday "to organise props", and his drama club was rehearsing a musical with silver costumes. He might have borrowed Marvin for the show, I thought — but it felt too obvious, almost like a trick.

💡 The deduction

Then I remembered the paint. The art club had been repainting scenery in the room next door all week. Their youngest member, Riko, wears size 36 shoes — and borrows the caretaker's key to fetch brushes.

I found Riko in the art room, standing next to Marvin the Moose. The old moose was wearing a freshly painted golden crown.

"The costume looked so tired and grey," Riko explained, blushing. "I wanted to surprise everyone at the final. I must have forgotten to leave a note — sorry!"

🏆 Case closed

On Saturday, Marvin marched into the sports hall with his golden crown shining, and the crowd went wild. Sometimes a mystery has no villain — just a shy artist with a good heart.

Riko didn't get into trouble. In fact, the head teacher praised the golden crown in Monday's assembly, and the drama club asked Riko to help paint their scenery for the musical. Our next issue of the school paper sold out within a day — everyone wanted to read how the mystery had been solved.

And the silver sequin? It can't have come from Riko, who never wears sequins. It was a red herring — it had probably fallen from a drama costume weeks earlier.

Next time something strange happens at school, I'll be ready — with my notebook, my camera, and a healthy suspicion of any clue that seems a little too obvious.

A true detective checks every clue, even the ones that lead nowhere.

🔧 Grammar — Modals of Deduction 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. For each quiz item, student says the answer aloud BEFORE you click it — and says HOW SURE they are (100%? 50%?).

✅ SURE — must have / can't have

modal + have + past participle

😤 ~100% sure it happened:
The thief must have had a key. 🔑

🙅 ~100% sure it did NOT happen:
Alicia can't have taken it — thirty people saw her.

🔑 "couldn't have" = "can't have"

🤔 POSSIBLE — might / may / could have

modal + have + past participle

⚖️ ~50% — a real possibility:
Dev might have borrowed the costume.
The sequin could have fallen weeks earlier.

🔑 Negative: might not have (NOT "could not have" — that means impossible!)
💡 The detective scale: can't have ❌ ←——— might have 🤔 ———→ must have ✅
Evidence decides where you stand. No evidence? Stay in the middle with might have.

💡 Hungarian tip: Hungarian says the certainty with a separate word — "biztos(an) elvitte", "lehet, hogy elvitte". English packs it INTO the verb: must have taken, might have taken. And it's always must HAVE + 3rd form — never "must had".

🎨 Create — My 60-Second Mystery 10 minStep 6
📹 Zoom: drop THIS page's link in chat — student opens it in their own browser and types here while sharing their screen. You watch and ask detective questions.

Invent your own mini-mystery! Pick your ingredients, write the clues and deductions — then read it to your teacher, who has to guess the solution. 🕵️

First, tap 2–3 story ingredients:

❓ The strange situation (what is mysterious?)
✅ One deduction you are SURE about (must have)
🤔 Two possible explanations (might have / could have)
❌ One impossible explanation (can't have)
🎭 The REAL solution (keep it secret until the teacher guesses!)
🗣️ Now perform it! Read your mystery and deductions out loud — but NOT the solution. Your teacher gets three guesses, each starting with "You must have… / Someone might have…". Then reveal!
⏭ SKIP-IF-SHORT: write only the situation + one must have + one can't have; the rest becomes homework.

Teacher note — Step 6

When you guess, deliberately use one WRONG form ("you must have took…?") and see if the student catches it — catching the teacher is huge fun and proves mastery. Push for full modal + have + participle chunks in their spoken reveal, not just "maybe the cat did it".
