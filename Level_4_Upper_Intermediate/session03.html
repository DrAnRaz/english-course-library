How to play:

1. Your teacher secretly picks an object, person or place and defines it WITHOUT saying its name: "It's a thing which lives in your pocket and whose battery always dies." 📱

3. Swap roles. Your definitions must start with "It's a person who… / a thing which… / a place where…"

No score here — the sillier the definitions, the better! 🤪

💡 Kids Whose Ideas Changed Your Day

Do you think inventions only come from old scientists in white coats? Think again. Some of the objects that you use every single day were dreamed up by people your age.

Here are four inventors who never waited for permission — and whose ideas are probably lying within two metres of you right now.

🍧 The frozen accident

In 1905, an eleven-year-old called Frank Epperson mixed a fruit drink on his porch and forgot about it — on a night when the temperature dropped below freezing. The next morning he found a frozen block, which he pulled out by its wooden stirring stick.

Frank, whose friends went crazy for the icy treat, kept making it for years. As an adult he patented his "frozen ice on a stick", and the ice pop that you eat every summer was born — from a lucky mistake.

There is a lesson hiding in that porch, which scientists repeat all the time: don't throw away the experiments that go wrong. One of them might turn out to be delicious.

🤸 The bouncing teenager

George Nissen was a sixteen-year-old gymnast who loved watching circus acrobats fall into safety nets. He asked himself a simple question: what if the net could bounce you back up?

In his parents' garage, which became his first laboratory, he built a prototype from canvas and rubber tyres. Today we call his bouncy invention the trampoline.

Nissen, whose talent for showmanship matched his engineering, spent years improving the design and demonstrating it anywhere people would watch. He even performed with a kangaroo, an animal whose enormous jumps made the perfect advertisement.

👆 Reading in the dark

Louis Braille, who lost his sight at the age of three, was only fifteen when he created something world-changing. He designed an alphabet of raised dots, which blind readers could feel with their fingertips.

The system that he invented in 1824 is still used today, in almost every language on Earth. Not bad for a teenager whose teachers thought his idea was unnecessary.

The idea did not appear from nowhere. Braille started with a military code which soldiers used to read messages in the dark, and simplified it until fingertips could race across a page. Blind students, whose lessons had depended on heavy raised-letter books, could finally write as fast as they read.

📺 The boy in the potato field

Finally, meet Philo Farnsworth, a fourteen-year-old farm boy whose daily job was ploughing potato fields. The long straight lines, which he stared at for hours, gave him a wild idea: a picture could be drawn line by line with electrons.

That idea, which he first sketched for his science teacher, became a key part of early television. The screen that you watched last night can be traced back to a homework diagram.

✨ Your turn

Every inventor in this article was simply a person who noticed something small and asked "what if?". The next everyday genius, whose invention we can't even imagine yet, might be reading this exact sentence right now.

So keep a notebook, which is cheaper than a time machine and far more reliable. Write down every strange question that pops into your head. Somewhere among the silly ones, there may be an idea whose time has come.

🔧 Grammar — Defining vs Non-Defining Relative Clauses 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. For each quiz item, student says the answer BEFORE you click — and shows "commas" with two finger-hooks ✌️ in the air when needed.

🎯 DEFINING — no commas

Tells us WHICH one — you can't delete it.

The system that he invented is still used.
Students who ask questions learn faster.

🔑 who / which / that / whose / where
🔑 Object pronoun can VANISH: the screen (that) you watched

💬 NON-DEFINING — commas!

Adds extra info — you could delete it.

Louis Braille, who lost his sight at three, was fifteen.
The garage, which became his lab, was tiny.

🚫 NO "that" after a comma!
🚫 The pronoun can NEVER vanish here.
💡 The delete test: cover the clause with your hand. Do you still know exactly which person/thing the sentence means? YES → extra info → commas. NO → it's defining → no commas.
"My brother who lives in Szeged is a chef" = I have several brothers. "My brother, who lives in Szeged, is a chef" = just one!

💡 Hungarian tip: Hungarian always keeps the pronoun (aki, ami, amely), but English often drops it in defining object clauses: "the song (that) I heard". And whose is not only for people — a phone whose battery is dead is perfectly correct English!
