📖 Reading — The Audition: Leo's Diary 10 minStep 4
📹 Zoom: share screen, take turns — one diary day each. Then student annotates every idiom she can find (Zoom → Annotate). All 12 are hiding in there!

Read Leo's diary about the school talent show. The idioms are in bold — try to feel what they mean from the story. Tap 🔊 to hear any part.

😬 Monday — the sign-up

When Ms. Okafor announced the school talent show, my best friend Sam signed me up without asking. "Relax," he said. "You've played guitar for five years — it'll be a piece of cake."

Easy for him to say. I had butterflies in my stomach for the rest of the day, and when people asked what I was going to perform, I said "a secret" — because honestly, I had no idea.

🎸 Tuesday — practice, sort of

I planned to practise all evening, but first I had to hit the books for a history test. By nine o'clock my fingers were tired and my brain felt like soup.

My sister said my playing sounded "like a cat in a washing machine". She was pulling my leg… I hope.

🤒 Wednesday — disaster?

I woke up feeling under the weather — sore throat, heavy head. Mum checked twice, because I skip school once in a blue moon, then made me soup.

Lying in bed, I finally had time to listen to the song properly, again and again. Weirdly, being ill was a blessing in disguise: I stopped panicking about my fingers and started actually hearing the music.

💪 Thursday — the comeback

My throat felt better, and I practised the song ten times without a single mistake. Even my sister admitted it sounded "almost like music". From her, that's a five-star review.

🥶 Friday — the audition

Backstage, I nearly got cold feet. The girl before me sang like a professional, and her costume probably cost an arm and a leg.

When I walked out, the silence was terrifying — so I told the audience my sister's joke about the cat in the washing machine. Everyone laughed, and it totally broke the ice.

Then I played, and my hands remembered everything my brain had forgotten. I didn't spill the beans about being ill all week — I just smiled and bowed.

🌕 Saturday — the results

Ms. Okafor posted the list this morning: I'm in the final! I was over the moon — I ran around the kitchen twice and scared the dog.

Next Friday is the real show. Am I nervous? Of course — but now I know the scariest part isn't the stage. It's the week before.

🔧 Language Focus — The Figurative Toolbox 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. For each quiz item, student names the answer out loud BEFORE you click it.

🔍 SIMILE & METAPHOR

Simile compares with like or as:
"She sang like a professional." · "As busy as a bee."

Metaphor says X IS Y — no like/as:
"The classroom was a zoo."

🎈 HYPERBOLE & PERSONIFICATION

Hyperbole = wild exaggeration:
"I've told you a million times!"

Personification gives things human actions:
"The wind whispered through the trees."
🔒 Idioms are FIXED phrases — the meaning ≠ the words, and you can't swap the words. It's spill the beans, never "spill the peas". Learn each one as a whole chunk, like a song lyric. 🎵

💡 Hungarian tip: never translate idioms word-for-word — in either direction! "Cost an arm and a leg" is NOT "egy karba és egy lábba került" (Hungarian would say csillagászati összeg) — and "Nem kolbászból van a kerítés" would totally confuse an English speaker. Always match the MEANING, not the words.

Teacher note — Step 5

Confusion hotspot: simile vs metaphor. Fix: "Can you see like or as? → simile. No? → metaphor." Point back at the reading: "like a cat in a washing machine" (simile) and "my brain felt like soup" (simile). If 6+/8, bank time for Step 6 production.
