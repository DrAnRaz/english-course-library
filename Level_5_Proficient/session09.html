🌍 One language, many voices — notes from a very confusing summer, by Mateo Fernandes, 15

Last summer, my family scattered me across the English-speaking world. I stayed with cousins in London and in Texas, video-called my cousin Wei Ling in Singapore, and gamed nightly with my friend Chidi in Lagos. I left believing I spoke English.

I came back knowing I speak an English — one of many.

🇬🇧 London: the pants incident

Disaster struck on day two. I told my cousin's whole football team that I'd forgotten to pack enough pants, meaning trousers. In Britain, "pants" means underwear, and eleven boys will never let me forget it.

Britain also queues. You do not stand near a bus stop; you join the queue, you respect the queue, you become the queue. Push in, and the politest nation on Earth will destroy you with one single raised eyebrow.

The vocabulary kept ambushing me in smaller ways as well. People said "cheers" for thank you, "fortnight" for two weeks, and "sorry" for practically everything — including the times I stepped on THEM. By week two I was apologising to furniture, which my cousin assures me is the true sign of fluency.

🇺🇸 Texas: bigger, louder, faster

Three weeks later, Texas. My aunt asked if I wanted chips, and handed me what Londoners call crisps; the "chips" I expected are "fries" over there. Same words, different planet.

Texans said "y'all" to everyone, took the elevator, threw out the garbage, and went on vacation in the fall. My London cousin, on our group call, pretended to faint.

Numbers tripped me as well. The date 05/06 meant the fifth of June to my London cousin and May sixth to my Texan aunt — we discovered this while planning the same video call, one hour and two continents apart. Nobody was wrong. That, I was slowly learning, was the whole point.

🌏 Singapore, Lagos and beyond

Wei Ling speaks flawless formal English at school — and glorious Singlish with friends: "Can lah, no problem!" That little "lah" adds friendly emphasis, and no dictionary can fully translate it.

Chidi greets me with "How far?" — Nigerian English for "how are you doing?" — and calls a traffic jam a "go-slow", which honestly beats the original. Meanwhile, my cousin in Mumbai says meetings get "preponed": moved earlier, the elegant opposite of postponed.

Wei Ling's roommate joined one call from Sydney and broke my brain completely. Australians shorten everything: afternoon becomes "arvo", breakfast becomes "brekkie", and a mosquito is a "mozzie". She cheerfully promised to ring me "this arvo, after brekkie", and I understood roughly half.

The strangest part? Within a week I had stopped translating and started collecting. My notes app now has a section for each country, and the Lagos page is the longest — Chidi donates a new expression every game night, and refuses to explain half of them on principle.

🧭 So who speaks it right?

Here is the fact that rearranged my brain: most English conversations today happen between non-native speakers. English has no single owner anymore; it is the world's shared lingua franca, and every region signs it differently.

Think about what that means in practice: for every native speaker of English there are roughly three people who learned it later — pilots, gamers, scientists, cousins. When Wei Ling, Chidi and I talk, no native speaker is present at all, and the English works perfectly.

At first, honestly, that discovery worried me. If everyone owns English, whose rules was I supposed to learn? Then I noticed the trick: speakers everywhere still understand each other almost perfectly, because the deep grammar barely changes — only the decoration does.

My teacher put it best when I got home. An accent is an address, not an error — it tells people where your English lives. Mine, apparently, lives everywhere now — and eleven London boys still text me about my pants.

🇬🇧 BRITISH ENGLISH

✏️ Spelling: colour, centre, realise, travelling
⏰ I've just eaten. (present perfect for recent events)
📅 at the weekend
🤝 Have you got a pen?
👥 The team are winning. (group = people)

🇺🇸 AMERICAN ENGLISH

✏️ Spelling: color, center, realize, traveling
⏰ I just ate. (past simple is fine)
📅 on the weekend
🤝 Do you have a pen?
👥 The team is winning. (group = unit)
➕ bonus participle: has gotten
💡 The golden rule: both systems are 100% correct — but be consistent. One essay, one flag. Mixing "colour" and "center" in the same text is the only real mistake.

💡 Tip: your spellchecker has a flag too! Check whether your document is set to English (UK) or English (US) before you trust its red lines.
