✍️ The scholar's toolkit — by Nadia Osei, study-skills columnist

Every year, thousands of brilliant students lose marks for a strange reason. Their ideas are excellent; their writing simply wears the wrong clothes. Today we fix that with two learnable skills: summary and register.

✂️ Summary: the art of shrinking

A summary is not a shorter copy of a text. It is the text's skeleton: the main claim, the key reasons, and absolutely nothing else. If your summary still contains the jokes, the examples and the statistics, it is not a summary — it is a hostage situation.

Try the look-away method. Read a paragraph, close your eyes, and write one sentence in your own words. Looking away matters enormously, because it forces genuine paraphrase and blocks accidental plagiarism — copying that you did not even notice yourself doing.

Length is the other trap. Professionals aim for roughly one tenth of the original: cut too little and you waste the reader's time; cut too much and the argument quietly collapses.

One warning about voice. A summary reports the author's opinion, never your own; the moment you add "which I find unfair", you have stopped summarising and started reviewing — an entirely different job.

🎩 Register: clothes for your words

Register is the level of formality you choose for a situation. Nobody wears pyjamas to a job interview, and consequently nobody should write "the results were kinda weird" in a science report. The ideas may be identical; the outfit decides how seriously they are received.

Formal English follows a few visible habits. It avoids contractions (do not, it is), swaps phrasal verbs for single verbs (investigate, not look into), and prefers precise nouns to vague ones (children, not kids).

Furthermore, it connects ideas openly. Words such as nevertheless and consequently are signposts; they tell your reader exactly which turn your argument is about to take.

📚 Cite it or lose it

One further habit separates scholars from students: citation. When you borrow an idea, name its owner — this is not politeness, it is armour. A cited claim says "check me"; an uncited claim whispers "trust me", and examiners never, ever do.

🚨 The trap on both sides

Beware, nevertheless, of over-dressing. Writing "I shall endeavour to elucidate" when you mean "I will explain" does not sound intelligent; it sounds like a robot butler. The target is formal and clear — never formal and foggy.

🧭 Your edge

Here is the genuinely good news: both skills are mechanical, and anything mechanical can be drilled. Summarise one short article every week and rewrite one casual message formally. Within a term, your writing will walk into the exam hall wearing a perfectly tailored suit.

🎩 FORMAL — the five moves

1. No contractions → do not, it is
2. Single verbs → look into → investigate
3. Precise nouns → kids → children
4. Open signposts → furthermore, nevertheless, consequently
5. Fewer "I feel" — more the evidence suggests

🚨 OVER-FORMAL — the robot butler

❌ "I shall endeavour to elucidate."
✅ "I will explain."

❌ "Utilise the aforementioned methodology."
✅ "Use this method."

🔑 Formal ≠ long. Formal = precise + neutral.
💡 The signpost map: adding → furthermore, in addition · contrasting → however, nevertheless · result → consequently, therefore. One signpost per turn — never three in one sentence.

💡 Hungarian tip: Hungarian formality lives mostly in WHO you're talking to (önözés). English formality lives in WORD CHOICE — you can say "you" to a professor, but you can't say "kinda". Shift the words, not the pronoun.
