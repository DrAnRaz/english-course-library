🎬 The calm before

The gym smelled of floor polish and nerves. Rosa was standing behind the curtain, bouncing on her toes, while the audience was still finding its seats. Somewhere out there sat three judges, half her school, and every single person she had ever wanted to impress.

She had been practising for this moment for six months. Every evening after dinner she had dragged her keyboard into the garage and played until her fingers ached. Nobody knew — not even her twin brother, Mateo.

Keeping the secret had not been easy. Twice Mateo had almost caught her — once when he came home early from football, and once when a neighbour asked, over the fence, who had been playing so beautifully all summer. Rosa had blamed the radio, and Mateo, to her enormous relief, had believed her.

There was a reason for the secrecy. At her first recital, aged eight, Rosa had frozen in front of forty parents; she had stared at the keys for a whole minute, then walked off. Tonight was her rematch.

⚡ The plan unravels

At 7:02 the lights dimmed and the host called her name. Rosa walked to the centre of the stage, sat down, and lifted the keyboard lid. That was when she noticed the problem.

The power cable was dangling from the side of the keyboard — unplugged. Someone had moved the equipment during the interval, and nobody had thought to reconnect it. Nine minutes of music, and no electricity.

She could hear her own heartbeat over the hum of the hall. In the front row, a small boy was swinging his legs and whispering to his mother. The judges were waiting, pens ready, faces polite and blank.

⏱️ Nine minutes

Her mind raced through her options, one useless idea after another. The nearest socket was four metres away, hidden behind a speaker the size of a fridge. The audience had started to murmur; a chair creaked; someone coughed.

Then Rosa remembered something. Years before the family had bought the keyboard, she had learned her first songs on her grandmother's old piano — the same piano that had been gathering dust at the side of this very gym since the spring concert.

She stood up, crossed the stage, and pulled off the dust sheet. The audience went completely silent.

Her hands were shaking as she lifted the lid. The keys were yellowed with age, and someone had carved a tiny star into the wood beside middle C. Rosa took one slow breath, the way her grandmother had taught her, and placed her fingers on the opening chord.

🌋 The twist

The old piano was scratched and slightly out of tune, and it was, in every way that mattered, perfect. Rosa played the whole nine minutes from memory — no cable, no screen, no safety net. Three rows back, Mateo was filming everything on his phone, mouth open.

When the last note faded, the silence lasted three full seconds — which, as any performer will tell you, is an eternity. Then the gym erupted.

People were standing before the applause had even found its rhythm. One judge was clapping over her head; another had stopped writing entirely. Rosa stood, bowed the way she had practised in the garage mirror, and walked off on legs that had apparently stopped belonging to her.

🎞️ After

Rosa didn't win; a year-seven girl with a diabolo took first prize, and honestly, she deserved it. Mateo found his sister backstage. "You never told me you could play," he said. Rosa shrugged, grinning. "You never asked."

Later, the caretaker admitted that he had unplugged the keyboard to charge the disco lights. Rosa thanked him — without his mistake, she said, the best moment of her year would never have happened.

Mateo's video, by the way, has forty thousand views. But that is another story.

Quiz answers: 1) the caretaker had unplugged it to charge the disco lights · 2) six months · 3) she had frozen at a recital aged eight · 4) her grandmother's old piano · 5) someone moved the equipment happened FIRST — "had moved" (past perfect) marks the earlier event; Rosa "noticed" (past simple) came after. 🔧 Grammar — The Four Narrative Tenses 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. For each quiz item, student says the answer AND its camera-job ("background / event / rewind") BEFORE you click.

🎥 THE CAMERA — now of the story

Past simple = the events, one after another.
The lights dimmed. She walked on. She noticed the cable.

Past continuous = the background scenery, already in motion.
The audience was finding its seats. Mateo was filming.

⏪ THE REWIND — before the story

Past perfect = an earlier event that explains now.
Someone had moved the equipment.

Past perfect continuous = a long activity before the story.
She had been practising for six months.
💡 The director's mix — one sentence, four jobs:
Rosa was standing backstage (scenery) when the host called her name (event); she had been practising for months (long rewind), but nobody had told her about the cable (rewind).

💡 Hungarian tip: Hungarian marks "earlier past" with words like már and korábban, not with the verb. English hides the time-machine INSIDE the verb: had moved = "korábban elmozdította". If you can add "already/beforehand" in Hungarian, you probably need past perfect in English.

🎨 Create — Build Your Opening Scene 10 minStep 6
📹 Zoom: drop THIS page's link in chat — student opens it in their own browser, shares their screen, and writes live while you watch and coach.

Time to direct your own opening scene — the first paragraph of a story that makes a reader NEED page two. 🎬 Pick your ingredients, then build the scene layer by layer.

First, tap 2–3 story ingredients:

🎥 LAYER 1 — The scenery (2 sentences, past continuous: what was already happening?)
⚡ LAYER 2 — The event (past simple: the moment everything changed)
⏪ LAYER 3 — The rewind (past perfect: the secret or backstory that explains it)
🧗 LAYER 4 — The cliffhanger (one short line that stops the reader breathing)
🗣️ Now perform your scene! Read all four layers aloud like a film trailer — slow down before the cliffhanger, then stop dead. Teacher identifies each tense by ear. 🎙️
⏭ SKIP-IF-SHORT: write only Layers 1–2 in the lesson; Layers 3–4 become homework.

Teacher note — Step 6

Coach the LAYER JOBS, not just accuracy: if Layer 1 arrives in past simple, ask "Is the camera moving yet, or is this the scenery?" Push for one vivid sensory detail in Layer 1 (sound or smell beats sight). Star move: have the student read the scene once WITHOUT Layer 3, then with it — feel how the rewind creates depth.
