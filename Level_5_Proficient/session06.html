⚡ Warm-Up — "I Take Your Point, But…" 5 minStep 2
📹 Zoom: pure talking game, no screen yet. Use the 👏 reaction every time the starter phrase is used correctly.

How to play:

1. Your teacher makes a silly claim: "Cats would be better students than dogs." 🐱
2. You must disagree — but EVERY answer has to start with "I take your point, but…"
3. Three claims, three comebacks. Then swap roles: your silly claim, teacher's polite comebacks!

The rule: first agree with SOMETHING, then attack. It feels strange — that's the point. No score! 🎾

Teacher note — Step 2

Silly claims that work: "Homework should be done at 6 a.m." · "Pizza is a breakfast food." · "Phones should be banned for adults too." Push past one-word concessions: "I take your point that cats are independent, but independence isn't the same as doing your homework." Model one full example first.

🏛️ The motion

Every year, Millbrook Academy's debate final fills the hall. This year's motion: "This house believes homework should be abolished." Speaking for the motion, Tomas; against it, Farida.

What follows are the key moments from the transcript, with notes on the moves each speaker made. Watch how the winners argue: rarely with volume, almost always with structure.

🌿 Opening moves: the hedge

Tomas began carefully: "The evidence suggests that homework, in its current form, tends to add stress without adding much learning." Notice what he did not say: "Homework is useless."

That caution is called hedging — softening a claim so it can be defended. "Suggests", "tends to", "in its current form": each phrase gives Tomas room to move when attacked. A hedged claim bends; an absolute claim snaps.

🤝 The concession trick

Farida's reply surprised the audience: "I'll concede that badly designed homework exists — we've all suffered through pointless worksheets." A debater agreeing with her opponent? Exactly.

This is concession, and it is a power move, not a surrender. By granting the weakest version of Tomas's point, Farida looked fair and reasonable — and earned the right to attack the strongest version.

"But," she continued, "abolishing homework because some of it is bad is like banning books because some of them are boring." The hall laughed; the judges wrote quickly.

⚔️ The rebuttal

A rebuttal answers the opponent's actual argument, not a convenient copy of it. Tomas's next move showed the difference.

"My opponent says homework teaches independence," he said. "But notice that she offered no evidence — only the assumption that suffering builds character. Finland assigns among the least homework in Europe and still tops the learning surveys."

He attacked the argument's support, not the person, and replaced it with evidence of his own. That is textbook rebuttal: locate the weak plank, press on it, then build your own.

Compare that with the lazy alternative, the strawman: inventing a weaker version of your opponent's claim and defeating that instead. "So you want students to do nothing all evening?" would have been a strawman — Farida never said that.

⚖️ What the judges said

The chief judge's feedback afterwards is worth memorising. "We don't reward certainty," she said. "We reward speakers who know exactly how strong each of their claims is — and say so."

She singled out the phrases that scored: "to some extent", "the evidence points towards", "while it's true that". Beginner debaters fear these phrases sound weak. The judges hear the opposite: control.

Her final advice went beyond debating. "You will spend your life disagreeing with people you must keep working with. Concede honestly, hedge precisely, rebut the argument and never the person — and disagreement becomes collaboration."

Farida won by two points. Tomas, gracious in defeat, was heard practising for the rematch on the bus home: "Admittedly, she was better today. But…"

Teacher note — Step 4 + answer key

Quiz answers: hedging phrases ("suggests", "tends to", "in its current form") · to look fair and earn the right to attack the strongest version · evidence · defeating an invented weaker version of the claim · how strong each claim is. Open Q1 key: an absolute claim dies from ONE counter-example; a hedged claim already contains its exceptions, so attacks slide off — precision, not weakness. 🔧 Focus — Hedge, Concede, Rebut 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. Student answers aloud in full debater voice BEFORE you click — posture counts!

🌿 HEDGE — armour for your claims

Soften the claim until it's defensible.

arguably · tends to · the evidence suggests · to some extent · in most cases

❌ "Homework is useless."
✅ "Homework, as currently designed, is arguably less useful than we assume."

🤝➡️⚔️ CONCEDE, THEN REBUT

Agree with the weak part, then strike the strong part.

Admittedly, … · While it's true that…, … · I take your point about X; however, …

"While it's true that phones distract, banning them ignores their learning uses."
💡 The champion's combo — one sentence, three moves: "While it's true that uniforms limit self-expression (concede), the evidence suggests (hedge) they reduce morning stress — so the real question is design, not existence (rebut)."

💡 Hungarian tip: direct Hungarian disagreement ("Nem értek egyet") translates as "I disagree" — grammatically perfect, socially a cannonball. English pads it: "I'm not sure I agree", "I see it a bit differently". The padding isn't dishonesty — it's the price of keeping the conversation open.

Watch for the classic C1 trap: hedging EVERYTHING until nothing is claimed ("It could maybe possibly be argued…"). Rule: one hedge per claim is control; three is fog. If they score 7+/9, go straight to Step 6 — the mini-speech is where today pays off.
