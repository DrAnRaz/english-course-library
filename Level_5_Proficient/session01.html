🗞️ The Persuasion Files — one student, two portraits

Last month, our school magazine ran a quiet experiment. Two reporters interviewed the same new student, Dario, and each wrote a short profile of him. Both writers were completely honest — neither invented a single fact.

✍️ Two honest portraits

The first profile called Dario "confident, determined and refreshingly direct." It said he "asks challenging questions" and "sticks to his opinions." Readers loved him; three people suggested him for student council.

The second profile described identical behaviour in different words. Here Dario was "arrogant, stubborn and blunt" — a boy who "interrogates his teachers" and "refuses to change his mind." Readers found him exhausting. Same student, same facts, same readers.

The editors printed both profiles side by side, and the corridor debate lasted a week. The most common reaction, one Year 10 student admitted, was mild panic: "If two honest articles can do that to one boy, what has every other article I've ever read been doing to me?"

🧲 Why the words won

So what happened? Every word carries two layers of meaning: its denotation — the plain dictionary definition — and its connotation, the emotional shadow it drags behind it. "Determined" and "stubborn" both denote unwilling to change, but one shadow is golden and the other is grey.

Linguists call these loaded words, and they steer readers like invisible hands. Advertisers exploit them constantly: a menu says "hand-crafted", never "home-made by somebody's uncle". Estate agents describe a tiny flat as "cosy" and a crumbling one as "full of character".

Sports commentary runs on the same fuel. When our team keeps the ball for ten patient passes, the commentator admires their "controlled build-up play"; when the rival team does exactly the same thing, it becomes "sterile passing that puts the crowd to sleep". One behaviour, two shadows — and most listeners never notice the switch happening in their own heads.

🎚️ Register: the other secret dial

Connotation is only half the machine. The other half is register — how formal or informal your language is. "Commence", "start" and "kick off" share one meaning, but you would never tell your best friend that the film "commences" at eight.

Getting register wrong is like wearing a tuxedo to PE, or pyjamas to a wedding. The clothes are fine; the setting isn't. Skilled speakers slide along the register scale a dozen times a day without even noticing.

Notice that neither end of the scale is "better". Formal language is not clever language, and casual language is not lazy language. A thank-you text that says "I hereby express my gratitude" is just as wrong as a job application that opens with "hiya". The only question that matters is whether the words fit the room they walk into.

🕊️ The politeness machine

Register also explains euphemism — the art of softening. Companies don't fire people; they "let them go". Characters in old novels never die; they "pass away".

Euphemisms can be kind, but they can also hide the truth. When a politician calls a serious mistake "an unfortunate outcome", listen very carefully.

Our school does it too. Look at any report card: "has difficulty maintaining focus" means doesn't pay attention, and "is a lively contributor to discussions" often means won't stop talking. Teachers soften their words for a kind reason — but a sharp reader learns to translate them back.

🎯 The takeaway

Here is the uncomfortable part: you cannot opt out. Choosing "slim" over "skinny", or "youthful" over "childish", quietly signals your attitude whether you intend it or not. Genuinely neutral language is much rarer than most people think.

The good news? Once you see the machinery, you control it. Before you describe anyone — in an essay, a message, or a complaint — ask one question: is this word reporting the facts, or smuggling in my opinion?

Our two reporters shook hands afterwards, by the way. Each insisted, with a perfectly straight face, that their own article was "candid" — and that the other one was "biased".

💛 CONNOTATION — the attitude inside the word

Same denotation, different feeling.

😊 slim · thrifty · determined · assertive
😬 skinny · stingy · stubborn · bossy

🔑 Test question: "Would I be happy to hear this about myself?"

🎚️ REGISTER — the formality dial

Same meaning, different situation.

🎩 commence · due to · I regret to inform you
👟 kick off · because of · bad news, I'm afraid

🔑 Test question: "Who am I talking to, and where?"
💡 The writer's two-step: first pick the FACT, then pick the ATTITUDE and the SETTING.
Fact: he saves money. → To praise: "He's admirably thrifty." → To criticise: "He's painfully stingy." → In a formal report: "He is economical with resources."

💡 Hungarian tip: Hungarian plays exactly the same game — takarékos vs fukar, magabiztos vs beképzelt. The skill transfers; you just need the English pairs. Danger zone: bilingual dictionaries list near-synonyms WITHOUT their attitude labels.

You are a "spin doctor" — a professional word-chooser. 🎩 Invent a character (or use a book/film character), fix three TRUE facts about them, then write two honest portraits: one that makes readers love them, one that makes readers wary. No new facts allowed — only new words!

First, tap every word pair you plan to use:

📌 Three neutral FACTS about my character
😊 Portrait A — make us love them (flattering words only)
😬 Portrait B — make us wary (critical words, same facts!)
🗣️ Now perform both portraits out loud! Read A like an award speech 🏆, read B like a warning documentary 🎬. Your teacher will guess which single fact each loaded word hides.
⏭ SKIP-IF-SHORT: write only Portrait A in the lesson; Portrait B becomes homework.

Teacher note — Step 6

Police the "no new facts" rule strictly — that's where the learning lives. If Portrait B adds "she is mean", ask: "Which FACT does that come from? Which loaded word could carry that opinion instead?" Strong students: add Portrait C in formal register (a school report about the character) to combine both dials.
