🧩 The mystery of the perfect mistake

When Réka arrived at her exchange school in Manchester, her grammar was flawless. She had studied English for eight years and could explain the third conditional in her sleep. Her teachers back home would have been proud.

Yet within a week, her host brother Callum was grinning at her sentences. "You don't make a party," he said kindly. "You throw one. And rain isn't big — it's heavy."

Réka's sentences broke no grammar rules at all. They simply weren't what native speakers say.

🧲 Welcome to collocation

A collocation is a word partnership — two or more words that habitually travel together. English speakers make decisions, do damage, take risks and catch colds. Swap the partners around and every sentence turns strange, like cheese on cornflakes.

The trap is invisible because your first language has its own partnerships, and your brain translates them loyally. A German teenager "makes" homework; a Spanish speaker "has" heat; a Hungarian "does" a party. Each translation is faithful — and each one is wrong in English.

🎲 Why partnerships beat rules

Here is the uncomfortable truth: there is no rule. Nothing in logic explains why rain is heavy but coffee is strong, when both simply mean "a lot". Why do you keep a promise but break a record? Nobody decided; native speakers simply inherited the pairs.

Some partnerships are strict. You can be utterly exhausted but not "utterly tired" — utterly only likes extreme words. You can be bitterly disappointed or bitterly cold, but never "bitterly happy".

Linguists estimate that fluent speech is stitched together largely from these ready-made chunks. Your brain doesn't build "to be honest" word by word; it grabs the whole chunk off the shelf. That is why natives sound fast and effortless.

It also explains a strange experience every learner knows: you understand each word in a sentence, yet the whole thing takes a second too long to process. Your brain is assembling from single bricks while the native speaker next to you is snapping together whole pre-built walls.

⚔️ The make/do minefield

The most famous battlefield is make versus do. Roughly: make is for creating and deciding — make a plan, make a promise, make progress. Do is for work and duties — do homework, do the dishes, do your best.

There are exceptions, of course. English wouldn't be English without exceptions.

You make your bed, although it feels suspiciously like a duty, and you do someone a favour, although a favour is surely something you create. When Réka complained that this was unfair, Callum just shrugged: "Nobody said the pairs were logical. We simply know which shoe goes with which."

📡 Training your collocation radar

The fix is not memorising lists — it's noticing. When you read or watch anything in English, hunt in pairs. Don't record "decision"; record "make a decision". Don't record "disappointed"; record "bitterly disappointed".

Songs and subtitles are perfect hunting grounds, because they repeat. Hear "take your time" in three different choruses and the chunk stops being vocabulary — it becomes reflex. Ten noticed partnerships a week beat a hundred memorised ones a month, because a noticed chunk arrives with a real situation attached to it.

Réka started a "partnership notebook" on her phone. Three weeks later, Callum had stopped grinning — and had started borrowing her notes for his Spanish class.

Her rules were simple: whole chunks only, one line each, and a star next to any pair that surprised her. The starred ones, she noticed, were exactly the ones she never got wrong again.

🏆 The pay-off

Collocations are the difference between English that is correct and English that is real. Examiners reward them, listeners relax around them, and best of all, they make speaking easier — because half your sentence is already assembled.

So the next time it pours in Manchester, you'll know exactly what to say. The rain is heavy. The wind is bitter. And your English? Getting stronger by the day.

🔗 VERB + NOUN — the big four

make = create/decide → make a decision, make progress, make a mistake
do = work/duty → do homework, do the dishes, do your best
take → take a risk, take a photo, take a quick shower
catch / keep / pay → catch a cold, keep a promise, pay attention

🎨 ADJ + NOUN & ADV + ADJ

heavy rain / traffic — strong coffee / accent
fast food — but a quick shower

bitterly disappointed / cold
highly unlikely / recommended
utterly exhausted (utterly + extremes only!)
💡 The chunk shelf: never store a word alone — store it with its partner, like a matched pair of shoes. 👟👟
Not "decision" → but "make a decision". Not "disappointed" → but "bitterly disappointed".

💡 Hungarian tip: the classic traps are loyal translations — bulit csinál → throw a party (not "make"), nagy eső → heavy rain (not "big"), lekéstem a buszt → I missed the bus (not "lost"). Your Hungarian isn't the enemy — unexamined translation is.
