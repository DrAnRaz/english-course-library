How to play:

1. Your teacher fires 6 rapid questions at you (favourite place, worst food, a fear, a dream…).
2. Rule: you may NOT answer with "yes", "no" or "maybe" — full sentences only, 10 seconds each! 🔥
3. Then swap: YOU put your teacher on the hot seat with 3 questions of your own.

No score here — this is about answering under pressure, like a real interviewee. 🧠

🎙️ The student who interviews everyone — by Amara Diallo, The Northside Voice

Every school has a quiet legend. At Northside International, it is Dario Kovač, sixteen, the calm voice behind the podcast "Ten Honest Minutes". He has interviewed a firefighter, a chess grandmaster and — famously — his own headteacher.

This month, our magazine turned the microphone around. For once, Dario had to answer the questions. He agreed, on one condition: "Ask me the hard ones."

🔍 Why interviews?

"People assume it was journalism that attracted me first," Dario laughs. "Actually, it was shyness. Asking questions gave me a script — a safe, structured way to talk to absolutely anyone."

"What changed everything was one conversation with my grandfather. I recorded him describing his village, and he revealed things nobody in our family had ever heard. It was that afternoon that turned a hobby into an obsession."

🛠️ The craft behind the questions

Dario prepares obsessively. For a single ten-minute episode, he writes roughly thirty questions — and then abandons most of them. "The reason I write so many is not to use them," he explains. "It is to feel free enough to listen."

Listening, he insists, matters far more than asking. "What most beginners do is race ahead to their next question. What a good interviewer does is chase the unexpected answer — the one that wasn't in the plan."

There is one more ritual. At the top of every page of notes, Dario writes a single "forbidden question" — the one he is genuinely afraid to ask. "If I never ask it, the episode feels polite but empty. If I do ask it, it usually becomes the title."

😬 The awkward episodes

Not every recording goes smoothly. One guest answered every single question with one word; another cried, then made Dario promise to delete the whole recording — which he did, immediately. "All I could do was keep the silence comfortable," he remembers. "Never have I felt so useless — or learned so much."

🧭 Advice for future interviewers

So what should you do if you want to start? Dario's list is short and strict. Research your guest until you could introduce them without notes; ask follow-up questions, because "the second question is where the honesty lives"; and never fake curiosity, since people can hear it instantly.

"It isn't equipment that makes a good interview," he says, tapping his ancient, taped-together microphone. "What makes a good interview is attention. That, and the courage to ask the question everyone else is politely avoiding."

🔮 What's next

Episode fifty arrives next month, and the guest remains top secret. Dario allows our reporter exactly one clue before showing her the door: "It's someone in this building that I've wanted to interview for two whole years." Our money is on the school cook.

🔧 Focus — Cleft Sentences & Emphasis 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. For each quiz item, the student says the answer out loud with dramatic stress BEFORE you click it.

🔦 IT-CLEFT — spotlight one element

It + be + [spotlight] + that/who…
Pulls ONE piece of the sentence into the light.

It was the silence that scared me.
It was my grandfather who inspired me.
It wasn't equipment that mattered.

❓ WH-CLEFT — build suspense first

What-clause + be + [answer]
Makes the listener wait for the point.

What I love is the silence before an answer.
What a good interviewer does is listen.
All I want is one honest reply.
💡 Two bonus emphasis tools:
1️⃣ The reason (why)… is… → "The reason why I write thirty questions is to feel free."
2️⃣ Negative inversion → "Never have I felt so nervous." "Not once did he interrupt." (auxiliary jumps in front of the subject!)

💡 Hungarian tip: Hungarian shows emphasis just by moving the word before the verb („A CSEND ijesztett meg"). English word order is locked — so English builds a whole frame around the word instead. Same spotlight, different lamp.

🎨 Create — Your Dream Interview 10 minStep 6

You just got a press pass to interview anyone on Earth — alive, historical or fictional. Plan it like Dario would. 🎫

First, tap the topics your interview will dig into:

🎫 My guest — and why (start with a cleft!)
🔍 My three probing questions (not answerable with yes/no!)
➡️ One follow-up I'd keep ready ("the second question is where the honesty lives")
🔦 Now answer ONE of your own questions as the guest — with a cleft sentence
🗣️ Now perform it! Your teacher becomes your guest for 2 minutes. Ask your questions, chase one unexpected answer with your follow-up — and wrap up politely.
⏭ SKIP-IF-SHORT: plan only the guest + 3 questions and do the live interview; type the cleft answer as homework.

Teacher note — Step 6

Play the guest with SLIGHTLY evasive answers so the follow-up is genuinely needed — deflect once on purpose and see if she notices and pushes. Praise any spontaneous cleft or inversion out loud. If questions come out closed (yes/no), have her convert them live: "Do you like fame?" → "What does fame actually feel like?"
