🗞️ One match, two stories

Last month, Riverside School's football team lost the regional final 2–1. You might think there is only one way to report that. Yet the two local sites that covered the match told stories so different that readers could wonder whether their journalists had watched the same game.

📐 Spot the slant

The Riverside Herald ran the headline "Brave Riverside Falls to Controversial Late Penalty". Its report described a team that "battled heroically" and was "robbed" by a referee's decision "that many spectators questioned".

The Northgate Post saw things differently: "Northgate Cruise Past Nervous Riverside". Its version mentioned the penalty in a single sentence, calling it "a routine decision", and devoted three paragraphs to Northgate's "dominant" midfield.

Even the photographs disagreed. The Herald chose a Riverside player face-down on the grass; the Post chose Northgate's captain lifting the trophy under confetti. Neither image is false, yet each tells you how to feel before you read a word.

The captions finished the job. Under its photograph the Herald wrote "Heartbreak for the hosts"; under the very same afternoon the Post wrote "Champions at last". A reader who saw only the pictures and captions would already have chosen a side — without reading a single sentence of the reports.

🧰 The tricks to watch for

Neither article contains an outright lie. The bias lives in subtler choices: loaded language, selective omission, and framing. "Robbed" and "cruise past" carry an emotional charge that plain "lost" and "won" do not.

Omission is quieter but just as powerful. The Herald never mentions that Riverside's goalkeeper made two costly errors; the Post never mentions that the referee also waved away a strong Northgate appeal. Each outlet simply leaves out whatever weakens its story.

Interviews get selected the same way. The Herald quotes a Riverside parent calling the decision "a disgrace"; the Post quotes Northgate's coach praising his team's "composure". Dozens of people left that stadium with opinions — each outlet simply chose the voice that sang its tune.

Framing decides where the spotlight lands before a single word is judged. Open with the penalty, and the match becomes a story about injustice. Open with the midfield, and it becomes a story about superiority.

🧪 A quick experiment

Try this with tonight's news. Pick one story and find it on two different sites. Underline every adjective, every dramatic verb and every quotation — then compare.

Most students who try it report the same surprise: the facts overlap almost completely, while the feeling of each article differs wildly. The information is shared; the attitude is engineered.

One Year 9 class turned the experiment into a game: they stripped both match reports down to bare facts and compared lists. The two lists matched on eleven points and disagreed on one — the size of the crowd. Everything that had made the articles feel opposite was carried by choices, not by facts.

👓 Reading between the lines

Skilled readers treat every text as a set of decisions. Who wrote this, and what do they want me to feel? What has been emphasised — and, more importantly, what has been left out?

Notice the verbs, too. When a writer says a coach "admitted" something, we sense guilt; "explained" sounds calm and reasonable; "claimed" plants a seed of doubt. Same event, three flavours of meaning.

None of this means journalism is worthless. It means reading is an active sport: the honest reader checks a second source, questions emotional vocabulary, and asks who benefits from each version.

The skill works far beyond the news, too. Product reviews, match highlights, even the way a friend retells an argument — each is a set of decisions about what to load, what to leave out, and where to point the spotlight first.

So, who really deserved to win the final? The honest answer is that one report alone cannot tell you. That, perhaps, is the most useful lesson of all.

🔧 Focus — The Language of Implication 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. For each quiz item, student says the answer and a one-line WHY before you click.

✅ STATED — on the page

The text says it directly. You can point at the exact words.

"The team lost 2–1."
"The referee gave a penalty."

🔑 Test: could you quote it in court? Then it's stated.

🫥 IMPLIED — between the lines

The writer implies; the reader infers. The meaning is built from clues.

"Even Marco passed the test." → the writer expected Marco to fail.
"She's stopped copying homework." → she used to copy.

🔑 Test: nobody wrote it — but everybody understood it.
🧰 The bias toolkit — three tricks to name on sight:
1️⃣ Loaded language — emotional charge hidden in the word: "robbed" instead of "lost after a penalty".
2️⃣ Omission — the weakening fact quietly missing: the Herald never mentions the goalkeeper's two errors.
3️⃣ Framing — where the spotlight opens: open with the penalty → an injustice story; open with the midfield → a superiority story.
💡 The reporter's verb is never innocent: said = neutral · explained = reasonable · admitted = guilty · claimed = doubtful · insisted = desperate. One little verb quietly tells you whom to believe.

💡 Hungarian tip: Hungarian packs distance into one tiny word — "állítólag". English spreads the same doubt across choices: allegedly, reportedly, so-called, "claimed". Watch for them: each one is the writer stepping back from the claim.
