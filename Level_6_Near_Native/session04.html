📜 The Tide Clock — part one

On the morning Marisol arrived at her grandmother's house, the sea was pretending to be calm. It lay flat and silver, like a coin someone had pressed into the shore. Only the gulls seemed to know better, wheeling inland in long, worried spirals. Marisol had learned to read birds the way other people read headlines. Whatever was coming, they had heard about it first.

The house stood at the end of the lane, leaning slightly toward the water, the way old people lean toward a conversation they do not want to miss. Salt had chewed the paint from its shutters. In the front window hung a brass tide clock, its single hand frozen between HIGH and LOW. The lane itself was quiet — the kind of quiet that feels arranged. Even the bees moved as though they had signed an agreement.

🕰️ The clock

"It stopped the winter your mother left," Grandmother said, without being asked. She said it the way you might mention the weather. Then she carried Marisol's suitcase inside as if it weighed nothing at all. Grandmothers, Marisol suspected, carried heavier things than suitcases every day of their lives.

Marisol stayed on the porch a moment longer. She was fourteen, and she had already learned that adults hide their largest griefs inside their smallest sentences. The clock, she decided, had not simply stopped; it had been stopped.

🍞 The kitchen

Inside, the kitchen smelled of fresh bread and of something older — candle wax, maybe, or years. Photographs crowded the wall, but Marisol noticed the gaps at once: pale rectangles of wallpaper where frames had been taken down. Every missing picture, she guessed, had held the same face. She counted the gaps without meaning to: seven, arranged in a careful line, like windows onto rooms that no longer existed.

"You will have your mother's old room," Grandmother said. The words came out flat and careful, each one placed like a cup on a shelf that had held broken things before. Marisol said thank you. It seemed the safest of the available words.

🚪 The room

The room upstairs was clean — too clean — the bed made with sharp hospital corners. On the desk sat a second tide clock, small as a pocket watch, and this one was running. Its hand crept toward HIGH with a patience that felt almost smug. Someone wound this one, Marisol thought. Someone in this house wanted at least one clock to keep going.

Marisol watched it for a long moment. Somewhere below, the sea had given up pretending; she could hear it now, muttering its old complaints against the rocks. The ocean never forgets, she thought — it only waits politely.

She unpacked slowly, folding each shirt twice. At the very bottom of her suitcase, wrapped in a scarf, lay the letter her mother had pressed into her hand at the station, still sealed. Marisol set it beside the small clock and did not open it. She told herself she would open it when she was ready. She did not ask herself when that might be.

🕯️ Supper

At supper they spoke of safe things: the ferry timetable, the price of bread, a neighbour's noisy dog. Twice, Marisol almost asked about the photographs. Twice, the question folded itself up and crawled back down her throat.

Grandmother watched her not-asking with something that might have been approval, or might have been relief. A dog barked somewhere down the lane, twice, and the silence closed back over the sound like water over a stone. In this house, Marisol was learning, silence was not the absence of conversation. It was the conversation.

Later, drying the dishes, Grandmother hummed three notes of a tune and stopped, as though the song, too, had been put away in a drawer.

🌙 That night

That night the wind rose, and the old house creaked in its sleep like a ship changing course. Marisol dreamed of a silver coin sinking through dark water, turning as it fell. In the dream she reached for it, and the water was warmer than she expected. When she woke before dawn, the little clock's hand stood exactly at HIGH — and downstairs, faint but unmistakable, the frozen clock in the window had begun, after all these years, to tick.

💬 Your interpretation — write your answer

1. Water and tides return again and again in this excerpt — a motif. What might the tide suggest about this family's story? Support your idea with one quoted phrase.
2. Marisol places the sealed letter beside the running clock and does not open it. What does this action reveal about her — and why do you think the writer put the letter next to the CLOCK?

Teacher note — Step 4 + answer key

Quiz key: personification · the family's frozen grief after the mother left · the frozen window clock starting to tick · photos of Marisol's mother were removed · quietly perceptive/observant. Open Q1 model: the tide (going out and returning) mirrors the mother leaving and the possibility of return; "the ocean never forgets — it only waits politely" implies the grief/story will come back. Q2 model: Marisol isn't ready to face her mother's message — she postpones, but placing it beside the RUNNING clock hints time is moving again and the choice can't wait forever. Accept ANY defensible reading backed by a quote — the evidence habit matters more than matching your reading.

🧰 Today's three devices — spot them, name them

🎨 Imagery — word-pictures that appeal to the senses, so the reader sees, hears or smells the scene instead of being told about it.
"It lay flat and silver, like a coin someone had pressed into the shore" — a visual picture that makes the calm sea look man-made, unnatural.
"the kitchen smelled of fresh bread and of something older — candle wax, maybe, or years" — smell carrying memory: the house's past is literally in the air.
🕊️ Symbolism — an object that secretly carries a bigger meaning than itself.
The stopped tide clock — frozen "the winter your mother left", it stands for the family's frozen grief: time in this house stopped when she did.
The sealed letter — an unopened decision. As long as it stays sealed, Marisol can postpone the conversation she is not ready to have.
🌩️ Foreshadowing — a quiet early hint of what is coming later.
"Only the gulls seemed to know better, wheeling inland in long, worried spirals" — the birds warn us, before anyone speaks, that the calm will not last.
The little clock's hand creeping "toward HIGH" — the rising tide builds quietly all day towards the night when everything starts moving again.

✍️ How critics write about them

✅ THE CRITIC'S SENTENCE — claim + evidence + hedge

analytical verb + quoted evidence + careful hedging

The stopped clock suggests frozen grief.
The phrase "too clean" implies the room is preserved, not lived in.
The letter may symbolise a choice Marisol is avoiding.

🔑 Power verbs: suggests, implies, conveys, evokes, hints at, mirrors

❌ THE AMATEUR'S SENTENCE — feeling with no anchor

Opinion only, no quote, no caution.

"The clock part was sad."
"Grandmother is obviously hiding everything."
"I liked the ocean bits."

⚠️ Nothing wrong with feelings — but a critic shows where in the text the feeling comes from.
💡 The magic pattern: QUOTE it → NAME the technique → EXPLAIN the effect.
"The sea 'was pretending to be calm' — personification — which makes the setting feel dishonest, as if the whole place is hiding something."

💡 Tip: hedging ("may", "could", "seems to") isn't weakness — it's precision. Literature rarely has one answer, and near-native writers signal that they know it.
