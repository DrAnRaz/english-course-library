🧳 Some words refuse to pack their bags — by Priya, 15

Last month my aunt flew in from Lisbon and gave me a present that fits in no suitcase: the word saudade. She described it as the sweet ache of missing something — a person, a summer, an older version of yourself — while somehow enjoying the ache. English owns half a million words, yet not one of them sits in that exact shape. I have carried the word around for a month now, trying it on like a borrowed coat, and it fits feelings I did not even know I owned.

🗺️ A quick tour of the untranslatables

Every language hides treasures like this. Danish has hygge, the candlelit cosiness of doing very little with people you like very much. Japanese offers tsundoku, the guilty habit of buying books and letting them tower, unread, beside your bed. If you have ever bought a book for the person you were hoping to become, you understand tsundoku better than any dictionary ever could.

Spanish gives us sobremesa: that golden stretch after lunch when nobody moves and the conversation outshines the food. German, never subtle, contributes Schadenfreude — the small, shameful spark of joy when your rival trips over his own shoelaces. Hungarian speakers will recognise its twin, káröröm, instantly. It is oddly comforting that two languages, born a whole continent apart, both decided this grubby little feeling deserved a name of its very own.

🤔 So is translation impossible?

Here comes the twist: none of these words is truly untranslatable, because I have just translated every single one. It simply took me a sentence instead of a syllable. What refuses to travel is not the meaning — it is the compression, the way a whole culture folds a shared experience into two neat syllables. A translation, then, is less a swap than an unpacking: you take the tightly folded suitcase of a foreign word and shake all its clothes out across the bed — and yes, it does take up rather more room that way.

When a Dane says hygge, she is not defining anything. She is pressing a button that releases a thousand remembered evenings.

🐘 Meanwhile, English hides its own

Before we get smug, notice that English performs exactly the same trick — with idioms. When I mention the elephant in the room, no zoo is involved; I mean that everyone can see the problem and nobody will name it. When my brother calls our family drama a storm in a teacup, he compresses a whole verdict into five words: too much fuss, too small a cause. Notice that neither of us paused to explain ourselves; the phrase did the explaining, instantly, the way a shared joke never needs a footnote.

Try translating throw someone under the bus literally into another language and watch faces fill with horror. The bus is not the point. The betrayal is.

🏛️ References — idioms with a backstory

English also squeezes entire stories into a phrase. Call a free app with hidden malware a Trojan horse, and you have quoted a three-thousand-year-old war in three words. Say the goalkeeper is our team's Achilles heel, and you have borrowed a Greek hero's fatal weakness without a single history lesson. The whole point is economy: one borrowed name, and three thousand years of story arrive already assembled.

These references work like a secret handshake. Miss them, and a paragraph feels locked; catch them, and the writer winks at you across the page.

🗝️ The real skill

Near-native English is not about swallowing a dictionary. It is about knowing which button each phrase presses — that tongue-in-cheek signals a joke wearing a serious costume, that reading the room can rescue you before you even open your mouth. The dictionary hands you the words; only living inside the language teaches you which one the moment is quietly asking for.

So collect untranslatables the way other people collect stickers. Every one you learn is a small country you can suddenly visit without a passport. And when you find a feeling English has no word for — congratulations: you have reached the edge of the language, and edges are where the interesting things live.

🧊 RULE 1 — Idioms are FROZEN

An idiom is one unit, like a single long word.

✅ It's raining cats and dogs.
❌ It's raining dogs and cats.
❌ It's raining many cats and a dog.

🔑 Don't reorder it, don't "improve" it, don't translate it piece by piece.

🎚️ RULE 2 — Idioms carry REGISTER

Most idioms are conversational. In a formal essay they can sound like trainers at a wedding.

💬 Chat: "The plan was a hot mess."
📄 Essay: "The plan was poorly organised."

🔑 One idiom per paragraph is seasoning; five is soup made of salt.
🏛️ Cultural references — the backstory club: Achilles heel (one fatal weakness) · Trojan horse (hidden danger inside a gift) · Pandora's box (one action releasing endless problems) · catch-22 (a trap where each way out needs the other) · Midas touch (everything they touch succeeds) · Big Brother (constant surveillance, from Orwell's 1984).

💡 Hungarian tip: Hungarian idioms are frozen too — nobody says "kolbászból van a sövény". Your instinct for "you just don't say it that way" already exists; today you're installing the English version of that instinct.
