⚡ Warm-Up — The Pineapple Summit 5 minStep 2
📹 Zoom: talking game, cameras on. Raise a virtual placard (your hand) before speaking — chair (teacher) must call on you first.

How to play:

1. The chair announces an urgent global crisis: "Does pineapple belong on pizza?" 🍍🍕
2. You and your teacher take opposite sides — and negotiate.
3. Banned words: "no", "wrong", "bad". You must disagree using ONLY diplomatic language: "My delegation respectfully disagrees…", "We are not entirely convinced that…"

No score — the only rule is: stay diplomatic while defending pizza with your life. 🧠

Read Tomasz's weekend diary from his first Model UN conference. Tap 🔊 to hear it read aloud.

🏷️ Friday, 16:40 — badge on, nerves on

According to the badge on my blazer, I am no longer Tomasz from Year 10; I am "the distinguished delegate of Chile". My committee is forty teenagers pretending to manage the world's water supply. Nobody warns you how quickly the pretending starts to feel real.

The chair — a terrifyingly calm sixth-former named Ingrid — opens with the rules of procedure. Speak only when given the floor; address everyone in the third person; never, ever point.

📄 The problem on the table

Our topic is water security — who gets clean water when rivers cross borders. Chile's position paper took me two weeks and taught me the first truth of diplomacy: you may not demand anything; you may only "urge", "encourage" and "strongly recommend". The verbs are soft, but they carry knives.

🙋 Saturday, 09:30 — the resolution wars

The morning begins with a motion to open debate, and suddenly the room is a chessboard. Sweden proposes a draft resolution with twelve operative clauses; Egypt calls three of them "regrettably vague", which in committee language means "terrible". I rise to a point of order when a delegate speaks past his time — heart hammering, voice miraculously level.

🤝 Saturday, 14:00 — the unmoderated caucus

Then the chair announces an unmoderated caucus, and formality dissolves. This is the real marketplace: knots of delegates trading concessions like football stickers. "We will support your amendment on desalination funding," I offer Kenya, "if you back our clause on river monitoring."

Kenya narrows her eyes, consults her notes, and shakes my hand. Consensus, I am learning, is not agreement — it is the point where everyone is equally unsatisfied and equally invested.

🎙️ Sunday, 11:00 — the floor

On Sunday morning the chair pronounces the six words I have rehearsed all weekend: "Chile, you have the floor." Ninety seconds. I thank the committee, concede that our draft is imperfect, express "deep concern" about the funding clause — and then unveil the compromise we cooked up in caucus.

No one applauds in committee; that simply is not done. But Egypt, who had threatened to veto everything, quietly withdraws her objection, and that is better than applause.

🗳️ Sunday, 15:30 — the vote

Voting procedure is pure theatre: placards rise, the chair counts, the room holds its breath. Twenty-eight in favour, six against, six delegates abstaining — resolution adopted. Sweden hugs everyone; Egypt abstained rather than vote against, which I now understand was her final, face-saving concession to us.

My hands, I notice during the count, are shaking slightly. My notes, I notice afterwards, are covered in tiny doodles of rivers.

🌍 What I actually learned

Here is my confession: I arrived believing diplomacy was the art of talking. It is mostly the art of listening for what the other side cannot say out loud.

Nobody in that room ever said "you're wrong". They said "my delegation respectfully disagrees" — and somehow the disagreement stayed about water, never about us. Imagine if group chats worked like that.

🔧 Language Focus — The Diplomat's Toolbox 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. Student reads BOTH options aloud in a diplomat's voice before choosing — the sound is part of the lesson.

🪶 TOOL 1 — SOFTEN (hedging)

Wrap the demand in cushions:

would / might / may → "We would urge members to reconsider."
not entirely / somewhat → "We are not entirely convinced."
With respect,… → polite alarm: disagreement incoming.

🔑 The softer the wrapping, the stronger the demand can be.

🛰️ TOOL 2 — DISTANCE (depersonalise)

Remove the finger-pointing:

Passive: "Concerns have been raised…" (by whom? mystery!)
Impersonal it: "It is regrettable that…"
The delegation, not the person: "My delegation disagrees" — never "I think you're wrong".

🔑 Attack the problem, never the people.
🗝️ Decoding diplomatic English: "not entirely convinced" = we disagree · "regrettably vague" = badly written · "we note with deep concern" = we are angry · "a frank exchange of views" = we argued · "we will study the proposal" = probably no.

💡 Hungarian tip: Hungarian builds politeness into pronouns and verb endings (Ön, tetszik). English has no polite "you" — so it does the same job with grammar: would/might, passives and impersonal structures ARE the English "Ön".
