⚡ Warm-Up — Sell Me This Broken Umbrella 5 minStep 2
📹 Zoom: cameras on, no screen yet. Teacher plays the sceptical customer — use the 💰 reaction if you're "sold", ❌ if not.

How to play:

1. Your teacher names a hopeless product: a broken umbrella ☂️ · one left sock 🧦 · an empty jar 🫙.
2. You have 30 seconds to sell it. Any argument allowed — emotion, logic, pure confidence.
3. Teacher buys or refuses — and must say WHICH sentence nearly convinced them. Then swap roles!

No score here — just notice which TYPE of argument you reach for first. We'll name it in a minute. 🧠

Teacher note — Step 2

When you debrief, label their instinct: "You went straight for emotion — that's pathos" or "You listed features — logos". Selling the empty jar as "storage for your hopes and dreams" is pathos; "technically dishwasher-safe" is logos; "trust me, I own twelve jars" is ethos. This creates the hooks Step 3 hangs on.

Read Amara's speech — an original speech written for this lesson. Tap 🔊 to hear any part read aloud.

🎙️ The setup

Last term, a school cut Creative Hour — sixty weekly minutes of art, music, coding and drama — to make room for extra test preparation. Amara, 15, asked to address the school council. This is the speech she gave.

She had never addressed an audience larger than her own class before. She wrote four drafts, rehearsed in front of her mirror, her dog and one very patient neighbour — and then walked in holding a single index card she never once looked at.

🏛️ The opening

"Members of the council, teachers, friends — I stand before you not to complain, but to ask a simple question: what is school actually for? Is it for filling in the right bubbles on a test sheet? Or is it for building the kind of minds that invent, question and create?

I have three minutes. I intend to use every one of them. I have also brought evidence, because I know this council respects evidence more than applause.

❤️ The heart of it

Think of Priya, who discovered in Creative Hour that she could compose music — real music, music that made our head teacher cry in the good way. Think of Daniel, who barely spoke in class until drama gave him a stage and, with it, a voice.

And think of me, because I am one of these stories too. Coding club began as my Creative Hour experiment; it is now the reason I know what I want to do with my life. One hour did that. One hour a week.

I am not describing rare talents. I am describing what ordinary students do when someone hands them sixty minutes and says: make something.

🧠 The evidence

And if stories don't move you, numbers should. Schools that protect creative time report better focus, better attendance and — here is the irony — better test scores. Ask our own teachers, and they will tell you the lessons after Creative Hour were the calmest of the week. One study of forty schools found the same pattern, year after year: creativity and results rise together, or they fall together. The evidence does not whisper; it shouts.

We did not trade one hour of play for one hour of learning. We traded imagination for exhaustion.

🔁 The build

We are not asking for less learning. We are asking for deeper learning. We are asking for the hour that made learning feel like ours.

Give us the hour that taught Priya to compose. Give us the hour that gave Daniel his voice. Give us back our hour.

⚖️ Facing the other side

I know what some of you are thinking: exams matter. You're right — they do. But a school that produces perfect test-takers and empty notebooks has not succeeded; it has simply measured the wrong thing.

We can prepare for exams and protect creativity. Other schools do both. So can we. This is not a choice between art and algebra. It never was.

📣 The call to action

So here is my request, plain and simple: restore Creative Hour from next term. Pilot it, measure it, judge us by the results.

If focus drops, cancel it — you have my word we won't argue. But if it works, and it will, you will have given us something no test can: proof that this school believes in what we might become. Thank you."

🔍 Why it worked

The council voted eight to one to restore the hour. Look again and you'll see why: the speech opens with a rhetorical question, builds ethos ("I stand before you not to complain"), stacks pathos through Priya and Daniel, anchors logos with evidence, then hammers the ending with anaphora — it repeats the "Give us" demand three times — because three is the magic number of rhetoric.

Notice the antithesis too — "perfect test-takers and empty notebooks" — and the concession that disarms the opposition before it can speak. Amara didn't get lucky. She built it.

There is one more trick worth stealing. Count the sentence lengths in the final section: long, long, short. Great speakers save the shortest sentence for the biggest moment, because a short line after long ones lands like a drumbeat. And notice what Amara never does: she never insults the council, never raises her voice on paper, never asks for pity. She asks for a fair test — pilot it, measure it, judge us — which is exactly the language the council itself uses. Speaking your audience's own dialect is persuasion's quietest device of all.

🔧 Language Focus — The Device Detector 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. For each quiz item, student names the device out loud BEFORE you click — then says which of the three appeals it serves.

Every persuasive text runs on Aristotle's three appeals — and a toolbox of devices that deliver them:

🏛️ THE THREE APPEALS

Ethos — trust me (credibility, fairness)
"As someone who has lived this…"

Pathos — feel this (stories, imagery)
"Think of Daniel, who barely spoke…"

Logos — follow the logic (facts, evidence)
"Schools that protect creative time report better focus."

🛠️ THE DELIVERY DEVICES

Anaphora — repeated sentence openings
"Give us the hour… Give us the hour… Give us back our hour."
Rule of three — lists of exactly three
"invent, question and create"
Antithesis — two opposites, one sentence
"perfect test-takers and empty notebooks"
Rhetorical question — no answer expected
"What is school actually for?"
Concession — agree first, then pivot with "but"
"You're right — exams matter. But…"
Call to action — tell them exactly what to do
"Restore Creative Hour from next term."
💡 The persuader's recipe: hook with a rhetorical question → earn ethos → move with pathos → prove with logos → concede the strongest objection → end with anaphora + call to action. Amara's speech follows it beat for beat.

💡 Tip: three is rhetoric's magic number in English — "invent, question and create". Two feels unfinished, four feels like a shopping list. When in doubt, cut or grow your list to exactly three.

🎨 Create — Your 60-Second Speech 10 minStep 6

Build a mini-speech with the persuader's recipe. Pick a cause you could argue with a straight face. 🎤

First, tap your cause:

🪝 The hook — open with a rhetorical question + one line of ethos
❤️🧠 The body — one pathos story + one logos argument
📣 The finish — anaphora (repeat your opening words 3×) + a clear call to action
🗣️ Now deliver it! Stand up, camera on, 60 seconds, no reading in a monotone — pause before your anaphora, slow down for the call to action. Your teacher votes like the council: pass or veto, with reasons.
⏭ SKIP-IF-SHORT: write only the hook and the finish; improvise the body while speaking.

Teacher note — Step 6
