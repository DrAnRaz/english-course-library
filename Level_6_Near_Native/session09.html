🎧 Meet the show — by Leila, 14

Every Sunday, my cousin Dawit and I record "Static & Sundays", a podcast about the strange questions nobody asks out loud. We have 214 subscribers, one squeaky chair, and a production process we take extremely seriously. Here is how an episode actually gets made. Nobody taught us any of this; we learned it one embarrassing episode at a time, mostly by noticing what made us skip other people's shows.

🪝 Minute zero: the cold open

We never start with "Hello and welcome". We start with a cold open — a slice of the episode's most dramatic moment, played before any introductions: "So the octopus has three hearts, and honestly, that explains everything about my week."

That is the hook. Podcast wisdom says you have about twenty seconds before a listener's thumb drifts towards another show; a good cold open buys you a full minute. A minute does not sound like much until you realise it is the entire distance between a listener and a bounce.

📝 The script that doesn't sound like one

People assume we improvise everything. In truth, every episode has a script — but it is written for the ear, not the eye: short sentences, contractions everywhere, questions that pull the listener forward. We read every line out loud before we keep it, because a sentence can look perfect on the page and then collapse the moment it reaches the mouth.

We also mark spots for ad-libs, where Dawit is legally allowed to wander off script for fifteen seconds. His banter is the show's engine; my job is steering it back with a segue: "Speaking of disasters — the news segment." The trick with a segue is to make the swerve feel deliberate, as if we had always meant to end up exactly there.

🎙️ Recording day

We record inside a blanket fort, because blankets swallow echo. Each of us records a separate track, so if I laugh over Dawit's punchline, the edit can rescue it. When a take dies, we don't stop — we clap once, and the spike in the waveform marks the crime scene for later.

Our gear is humble: two borrowed microphones, a laptop older than either of us, and a "RECORDING — QUIET PLEASE" sign that our cat has never once respected. It turns out the script matters more than any of it. We spent our first month convinced we needed better microphones; we spent our second month learning that a boring script sounds boring on any equipment.

✂️ The edit: where the magic hides

Editing is honest work: we cut every "um", every dead pause, every joke that only the two of us found funny. A jump cut in audio is forgivable; boredom is not.

We always rescue one sound bite — a quotable ten-second moment — and post it as a teaser clip. That clip recruits more listeners than anything else we do. Choosing that one clip is agony, because the ten seconds that hook a stranger are almost never the ten seconds we personally love the most.

📣 Shipping it

Before upload, I write the show notes: a two-line summary, timestamps, and the links we mentioned. The outro lands the same two beats every week — a thank-you, then a call to action: "If the octopus facts improved your week, share the show with exactly one human." That single-person ask works far better than begging a whole audience; one real listener telling one friend is how the show has genuinely grown.

And we always end on a cliffhanger for next Sunday. Dawit insists; he is usually right.

Upload time is six o'clock sharp, every single week. Consistency, Dawit read somewhere, is the only growth hack that actually works.

🌟 Why this matters for your English

Here is the secret we discovered by accident: scripting a podcast is the best English lesson we have ever had. Writing for the ear forces you to sound like yourself — only sharper. Suddenly grammar stopped being a rulebook and became a set of dials we could actually turn.

When every sentence has to survive being spoken aloud, lazy phrases get exposed in seconds. Try it once, and you will never write a school presentation the same way again.

🔧 Language Focus — Writing for the Ear 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. Student must read BOTH options of each quiz item ALOUD before choosing — their own ear is the judge.

🎙️ SOUNDS SPOKEN — do this

Contractions: "It's wild, isn't it?"
Short sentences. Then a longer one for rhythm.
Talk TO them: "You know that feeling when…"
Signposts: "Here's the thing…", "First up…", "Stay with me…"

🔑 Test: could you say it to a friend without sounding like a robot?

📄 SOUNDS WRITTEN — avoid on air

Heavy openers: "Notwithstanding the aforementioned factors,…"
Noun-stacking: "the implementation of the utilisation of…"
Endless sentences the listener can't rewind.
Lists of seven items — ears hold three.

🔑 The eye can re-read; the ear gets ONE chance.
🥁 The rule of the ear: one idea per sentence · three items per list · a question every few lines to re-hook the listener · and read EVERYTHING aloud before you call it finished.

💡 Tip: this is why your favourite hosts sound "effortless" — the effort happened in the script. Effortless is a style, and styles can be learned.
