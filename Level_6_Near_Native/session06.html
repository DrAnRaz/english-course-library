🖊️ The red pen finds me

Nobody plans to become an editor. It happens the way most fates do: quietly, and then all at once. One Tuesday I fixed a friend's history essay; by Friday, half my class had discovered that I cannot walk past a broken sentence any more than a dog can walk past a dropped sausage.

Now I edit our school paper, The Weekly Racket. Every Thursday, articles arrive in my inbox like patients arriving at a small, understaffed hospital. Some need a plaster; some need surgery. A few, I am sorry to report, arrive beyond saving and must be sent back with flowers and a kind note. I keep the note short. It seems only consistent.

🔬 The usual suspects

After two years, I can diagnose the classic diseases on sight. There is the run-on sentence, which starts in the cafeteria and refuses to stop until it has visited every corridor in the school. There is its opposite, the fragment. Alone and shivering. Like this. Both diseases have the same cause, oddly enough: the writer stopped listening to the sentence. Read your work aloud and your own breath will tell you where the full stops go.

There is redundancy, my personal favourite, in which writers "descend down the stairs", make "future plans", and receive "free gifts". And there is the dangling modifier, a circus acrobat with no safety net: "Walking to class, the fire alarm went off." As far as I know, our fire alarm has never walked anywhere.

Worst of all is ambiguity, the shape-shifter. "She told her sister that her article was chosen" — whose article, exactly? The sentence knows, but it refuses to say, and the reader is left interrogating pronouns like a detective with no witnesses. I once spent ten minutes on that exact sentence before surrendering and phoning the writer. She knew instantly whose article it was. Readers, I reminded her, cannot phone.

🎯 Why precision matters

People assume editing is about rules. It is not, really — it is about mercy for the reader, who has one short life and should not spend it decoding "due to the fact that" when "because" was standing right there. The fancy phrase is not more intelligent; it is only longer. Intelligence, in writing, usually looks like restraint.

Precision is also about honesty. A vague sentence lets a writer hide: "mistakes were made" spreads the blame so thin that nobody has to taste it. A precise sentence — "I broke the microscope" — costs more courage and buys more trust.

My history teacher once handed back an essay with a single comment: "This paragraph is hiding something." She was right. I had written four foggy sentences because I had not done the reading, and the fog itself was the confession. I have never forgotten it. Now, when my own sentences start fogging up, I stop and ask what I am avoiding — the answer is usually the very thing I should write next.

✂️ The hardest cut

The cruellest part of the job is deleting a sentence its writer clearly loved. Editors call these darlings. Last month I cut a description of our goalkeeper as "a majestic, mildly damp eagle" — beautiful, useless, gone. The writer mourned for a week. Then he admitted the article read better, which is the closest an editor ever comes to receiving a medal.

But here is the secret every editor eventually learns: cutting is a compliment. You only sharpen a blade you intend to use.

😳 A confession

And yes — the rumours about the semicolons are true. I love them; I overuse them; I have made my peace with it. Every editor has one weakness, and at least mine is punctuation, not cowardice. My deputy editor collects em-dashes the way other people collect stamps; we monitor each other. It is a fair system, and it mostly works.

So send me your tangled paragraphs and your wandering modifiers. I will return them shorter, straighter, and slightly less damp than a majestic eagle — because good writing is not decoration. It is the shortest honest road between your mind and someone else's.

📹 Zoom: share screen. Student diagnoses each sick sentence aloud BEFORE you click — first the disease name, then the cure.

✅ THE EDITOR'S CURES

One idea per sentence. Full stop is free — use it.
Cut the echo: "revert back" → revert · "free gift" → gift
Rescue the dangler: the -ing phrase must describe the SUBJECT that follows.
Comma splice → semicolon (or full stop, or "and/so/but").
Say who did it: "I broke it" beats "it got broken".

⚠️ THE PRECISION TRAPS

its = belonging · it's = it is
fewer + countable (fewer mistakes) · less + uncountable (less noise)
affect = verb · effect = noun (usually)
who = subject · whom = object (after "to admire, to trust…")
only moves meaning: "I only lent it" ≠ "I lent it only to Kai".
💡 The editor's chant: READ it aloud → CUT what repeats → CHECK who is doing each action → ONE idea per sentence. If a sentence makes you breathe twice, it wants to be two sentences.

💡 Hungarian tip: Hungarian word order is famously flexible — the meaning survives the shuffle. English is stricter: position IS meaning, which is why dangling modifiers and a wandering "only" feel harmless in Hungarian but change the sentence completely in English.

Teacher note — Step 6 + answer key

The 8+ planted problems: 1) "Due to the fact that" → Because; 2) "which is new" redundant; 3) "revealed and shown" redundant pair; 4) comma splice "…about it, it was…"; 5) dangling modifier "Walking into the cafeteria, the smell…"; 6) "In my personal opinion, I think that" double redundancy; 7) "their" → "there"; 8) "in the future going forward" tautology; 9) "Lets" → "Let's" AND the missing comma: "Let's eat, students!" (callback to warm-up — celebrate loudly if they catch it). Model rewrite: "Because the new cafeteria menu appeared on Monday, it became the talk of the school. Walking into the cafeteria, I was hit by the smell of pizza. There are fewer options for vegetarians, though, which is not ideal. The head cook says the menu will be adjusted. Let's eat, students!" Grade their edit on diseases CURED, not on matching your wording.
