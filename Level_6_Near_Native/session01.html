🎬 One Tuesday, five voices — by Leilani, 15

Last Tuesday, at exactly 12:47, I dropped my lunch tray in front of the entire cafeteria. Spaghetti everywhere. Someone slow-clapped, and a Year 7 filmed it, obviously.

My writing teacher, Mr. Okafor, refused to let me suffer in peace. "Write it five ways," he said, "and you'll never fear a blank page again." So here it is: one small disaster, five completely different voices.

📰 Voice one — the news report

"At 12:47 on Tuesday, a Year 10 student suffered a catastrophic tray failure in the east cafeteria. Witnesses describe a scene of widespread marinara. Authorities have not ruled out gravity, and the student is expected to make a full recovery — though the same cannot be said for the garlic bread."

Notice the deadpan tone. The vocabulary stays solemn — suffered, witnesses, authorities — while the subject is ridiculous, and that gap is exactly where the humour lives. My grandmother read this version and asked, quite seriously, whether anyone had been injured. That is how convincing a straight face can be on paper: keep the vocabulary solemn enough and even spaghetti sounds like a national emergency.

🕯️ Voice two — the gothic novel

"The tray trembled in my pale hands, as though it sensed its doom. Time slowed; the fluorescent lights flickered like dying stars. Then — oh, cruel fate! — the spaghetti descended, as empires fall: slowly, and then all at once."

Everything here is exaggerated and lyrical. The sentences stretch out, the punctuation gasps, and the tray practically has feelings. I will admit I enjoyed writing this one far too much. Once you allow a lunch tray to possess a soul, the adjectives simply pour out of you, and stopping becomes the genuinely difficult part.

✂️ Voice three — the minimalist

"Tuesday. Tray. Floor. Silence, then laughter. I ate an apple standing up."

Five fragments, one complete story. Minimalism trusts the reader to supply the emotion, and that restraint somehow makes it hit harder. Notice what it leaves out: no adjectives, no excuses, no self-pity.

Strangely, this version took me the longest to write, which sounds absurd for eleven words. Cutting, it turns out, is far slower than adding, because every word that survives has to earn its place on the page.

📋 Voice four — the corporate memo

"Regarding Tuesday's incident: a comprehensive review of tray-handling procedures is now underway. Moving forward, all pasta-based items will be transported with two hands. We thank you for your patience during this challenging time."

This voice hides behind nouns — incident, review, procedures. Nobody drops anything; things merely occur. Passive, foggy phrasing does the heavy lifting, and responsibility quietly evaporates. Mr. Okafor claims that half the adult world writes like this every single day, and that learning to recognise the fog is a survival skill. Honestly, after reading three school newsletters in a row, I believe him.

🎤 Voice five — me, talking to my best friend

"Okay, so you know how I have zero hand-eye coordination? Yeah. Whole cafeteria. Whole. Cafeteria. I genuinely wanted the floor to swallow me — but honestly, by Wednesday it was my best story."

This is my natural register: contractions, fragments, repetition for drama. Notice genuinely and honestly — spoken English leans on these little sincerity markers. It sounds like me because the rhythm copies how I actually speak. When I read this version aloud, my actual best friend declared it so accurate it was slightly creepy — apparently I really do repeat words when I am embarrassed.

💡 What actually changed?

Read the five versions again and you'll notice something strange: the facts never moved. What changed was the distance. The news report stands far away; the gothic version drowns in feeling; the memo hides; the friend version leans close. Same spaghetti, completely different distances — like photographing one object from across the street, then through a window, then from two centimetres away.

Style, it turns out, is a set of decisions: which words, how long the sentences run, how near the reader stands. Change the decisions and you change the voice — on demand. You already do this, by the way: you talk one way to your grandmother and another way to your group chat. Nobody taught you that; you absorbed it. The only real difference now is doing it on purpose — hands on the dials instead of running on autopilot.

Last week I caught myself rewriting a two-line text message three different ways before sending it, purely to feel the difference between them. Mr. Okafor was right in the end. I haven't feared a blank page since. I fear the cafeteria instead.

🔧 Style Lab — The Register Dials 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. For each quiz item, student says the answer BEFORE you click — and names WHICH dial it turns.

Think of style as a mixing desk with four dials. Every text you'll ever write sets them somewhere:

🎚️ DIAL 1 & 2 — Words & Grammar

Word origin: everyday vs Latinate
buy → purchase · ask → inquire · put off → postpone

Agent visibility: active vs passive/nominalised
"I broke it" → "It got broken" → "A breakage occurred"

🔑 Formal English hides people behind nouns and passives.

🎚️ DIAL 3 & 4 — Rhythm & Distance

Sentence length: terse fragments vs flowing clauses
"Tuesday. Tray. Floor." vs "The tray trembled in my pale hands, as though…"

Distance: how close the reader stands
contractions, "honestly", direct address = close · impersonal "one", "it is regrettable" = far
💡 The C2 move: deliberate MISMATCH. Deadpan = formal dials + silly content. Mock-epic = gothic dials + tiny event ("oh, cruel fate! — the spaghetti descended"). Control the mismatch and you control the humour.

💡 Hungarian tip: Hungarian shifts formality mainly through grammar (tegezés/magázás). English has no "ön" — it shifts formality through WORD CHOICE and structure instead. Same grammar, different wardrobe: "Can you help me?" vs "Would you be so kind as to assist me?"
