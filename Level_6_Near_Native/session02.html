🌐 Do you speak internet? — by Mateo, 15

My grandmother texted me "lol" after telling me her cat was ill. She thought it meant "lots of love". She is not alone — and honestly, she's not even wrong, just twenty years late.

To be fair to her, she has a system: she signs every text with her full name, as though it were a letter, and she has used the same three emojis since 2019. There is something almost elegant about that consistency. She is not failing at internet English; she is speaking her own version of it.

Meanwhile my little cousin uses words I have to look up, and my teachers still say things nobody has said since 2015. Internet English is the fastest-moving dialect on Earth. A word can be born on a Tuesday, peak on a Friday, and be embarrassing by the following month.

📈 Where slang actually comes from

Most of today's slang isn't invented by apps; it's borrowed. Linguists trace a huge amount of it to African American English, drag culture and gaming communities, where it lived for years before going viral.

By the time a word reaches a cereal advert, its cool is basically gone. That's the life cycle: invention, adoption, explosion, cringe.

My favourite proof: the moment a bank uses a word in an advert, every teenager quietly agrees to retire it. Adults never receive the memo, which is why the word then survives another full decade — in staff rooms.

🗣️ Grammar with a wink

Internet English also bends grammar on purpose. "You coming?" drops words; "I can't even" stops mid-sentence; typing in all lowercase signals calm irony, while a full stop at the end of a text can read as quiet fury.

These aren't mistakes. They're tone of voice, rebuilt in text — punctuation doing the job your face usually does. Researchers have actually studied this: readers consistently rate the one-word reply "Fine." as colder than "fine" without the full stop. One small dot, an entire mood.

🔀 The real skill: code-switching

Here's what surprises adults: heavy slang users are often excellent code-switchers. The same student who types "no cap, that essay was a struggle" will write "I found the essay genuinely challenging" to a teacher — without blinking.

I watched my friend Priya do this live once. She sent "bruh this is tragic" to our group chat and, eleven seconds later, "Thank you for the feedback — I will revise the introduction" to our teacher. Same phone, same thumbs, two different outfits. Both messages were completely her.

Switching between registers isn't fake. It's the linguistic equivalent of owning both trainers and formal shoes, and knowing which event you're dressed for.

⚠️ When slang backfires

Slang is a membership card. Use it with the right people and it says "I'm one of you"; use it in a scholarship application and it says something quite different.

There's also the expiry problem: nothing ages a sentence faster than last year's catchphrase. A good test — would you say it to a friendly adult you respect? If the answer is "absolutely not", you've found the boundary.

🧪 A tiny experiment

This week I collected every abbreviation my family used in our group chat. My dad still writes "BRB", which nobody has typed unironically since about 2014, and my cousin ends serious sentences with the skull emoji, which alarmed my mum considerably.

Three generations, three internets. Each of us is fluent in our own layer and mildly confused by the others.

The strangest discovery came from my mum, who has quietly invented a hybrid: flawless grammar, full punctuation, and then one wildly outdated emoji doing all the emotional work at the end. Linguists probably need a name for that layer too. I suggest "parent formal".

💬 So, should you use it?

Yes — deliberately. Slang isn't broken English; it's English doing what English has always done: mutating, borrowing, showing who you are. Shakespeare coined new words constantly; teenagers in group chats do the same job, just faster and with more emojis. People panicked about Shakespeare's inventions too, by the way. The language survived. It always does.

The trick is control: know your audience, know the expiry date, and always be able to say the same thing formally. That's not selling out — that's being bilingual in your own language.

Fluency at the highest level doesn't mean sounding like a dictionary. It means choosing, every single time, exactly how you want to sound.

🔧 Language Focus — The Register Radar 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. For each quiz item, student answers BEFORE you click AND names the audience it fits.

Informal English isn't grammar-free — it has its OWN grammar. Learn the features, then choose them on purpose:

💬 INFORMAL toolkit — real features

Ellipsis: "(Are) you coming?" · "(I) can't complain."
Reductions: gonna, wanna, dunno, lemme
Discourse markers: like, literally, lowkey, tbh
Punctuation as tone: all lowercase = relaxed irony; "Fine." with a full stop = ice-cold ❄️

🔀 FORMAL switch — same meaning, new outfit

"can't make it" → "unable to attend"
"no cap" → "honestly / frankly"
"it slaps" → "it's outstanding"
"kinda mid" → "rather mediocre"

🔑 The C2 test: can you say EVERYTHING you mean in BOTH registers?
💡 The audience rule: register is chosen by the READER, not the writer. Group chat → slang builds belonging. Teacher email → standard English shows respect for their time. Unknown adult → formal until invited otherwise.

💡 Hungarian tip: Hungarian borrows English internet slang directly (cringe, flex, "lájkol"), but meanings drift between languages. Before using a term in ENGLISH, check what it means to native speakers now — not what it means in the Hungarian internet bubble.
