How to play:

1. Your teacher reads a fun statement: "Pizza is better than pasta." 🍕
2. You react FAST — 👍 or 👎 — then say ONE reason: "…because…"
3. After 5 statements, swap: YOU invent the statements, teacher reacts.

No score here — there are no wrong opinions, only missing reasons! 🧠

📰 The Big Question — should phones be allowed in class?

This month, our school magazine asked two students from Year 8 to share their opinions. Warning: they do NOT agree!

📱 Kenji says YES

In my opinion, phones are simply tools — like calculators or dictionaries. If we use them well, lessons get better.

For example, last month our science class used phones to film a plant experiment. We watched the video in slow motion and saw things we had missed. That was real learning.

Some people say phones only mean games and chatting. That's true sometimes, but the answer is teaching good habits, not banning everything. We don't ban pens because someone draws on the desk! In my class, we wrote our phone rules together, and it works.

🚫 Amara says NO

I see Kenji's point, but I disagree. Phones are designed to steal your attention. Every buzz pulls your brain away from the lesson.

Scientists tested this: students remember more when the phone is in another room — even when it is turned off! For example, in my old school, phone-free days felt calmer. People actually talked at lunch.

I'm not saying phones are evil. I think that school should be the one place where our brains get a real break. Your friends are right there, in the same room. Talk to them!

🎯 What happens now?

The magazine asked 60 students to vote. The result was 31 for Kenji and 29 for Amara — almost a perfect tie! Next week, the school council will debate the question.

What do YOU think? And more importantly — can you say it politely?

Teacher note — Step 4 + answer key

Quiz answers: a plant experiment · teaching good habits · students remember more when the phone is in another room · 31–29, almost a tie. Open 1: either side is fine — require "In my opinion… because…". Open 2: polite phrases in the text: "That's true sometimes, but…", "I see Kenji's point, but I disagree", "I'm not saying… I think that…". Point out that both writers attack the IDEA, never each other — that observation sets up Step 5.
🔧 Focus — Polite Disagreement 10 minStep 5
📹 Zoom: share screen. The student says each answer out loud with FRIENDLY intonation before you click.

✅ AGREEING

I agree. · Exactly!
I totally agree! · That's a good point.

Stronger with a reason:
"I agree, because dogs love people."

🛑 DISAGREEING — POLITELY

Soft starts:
I'm not so sure…
I see your point, but…
That's true, but…

Never: "You're wrong!" ❌ "That's stupid!" ❌
🔑 The debate sandwich: 1️⃣ RESPECT — "I see your point," 2️⃣ BUT — "but I think homework helps," 3️⃣ BECAUSE — "because we practise alone."
Respect + but + because = you sound smart, not rude.

💡 Tip: attack the IDEA, never the PERSON. "That idea has a weak point" ✅ — "YOU are wrong" ❌. Good debaters stay friendly and win anyway.

Teacher note — Step 6

Argue the opposite side gently — push just enough that the student NEEDS "That's true, but…" at least once. Count their polite phrases on your fingers on camera (goal: 3+). If they freeze, whisper a sentence starter — don't take over. If they get rude, smile and model "Fair enough — but…"
